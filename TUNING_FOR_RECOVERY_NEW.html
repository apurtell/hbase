1. Abstract

This document is a layer-by-layer capacity model for tuning HBase to absorb a sudden loss of an availability zone. The model is built bottom-up. Each layer of the stack is described, its configuration space is surveyed, recommendations are derived, and the resulting impact is then carried as a small set of constants and multipliers into the next higher layer. Bottleneck identification is an emergent property of composing those layers.

The reference workload is a 500-node cluster with 1,500 regions per RegionServer, suffering simultaneous loss of one third of its RegionServers (≈167 dead, ≈333 surviving), generating ≈250,000 region transitions and ≈1.5 million procedure state transitions over the recovery window.

The mathematical scaffolding is Little's Law, $L = \lambda W$, applied independently at each stage of the recovery pipeline, together with a small number of burst-concurrency lower bounds, one closed-loop throughput equation for the procedure store, and a bulk-service queueing analysis that bounds utilization on the procedure store under realistic service-time variance. Each tuning parameter is sized so that no single stage becomes the bottleneck at the target arrival rate.

The pipeline crosses three domains and eight layers. L1–L3 model HDFS (NameNode, DataNode write pipeline, DataNode read path); L4–L5 model the RegionServer (WAL writer, execution: split + open); L6–L7 model the master (procedure store, orchestration); L8 composes the per-layer drain times into a recovery-time bracket.

2. Workload Model

The workload model defines the layer-independent inputs that every higher layer consumes:

SymbolMeaningValue
$N$cluster size (total RegionServers)500

$f$fraction of cluster lost in an AZ outage$\tfrac{1}{3}$
$t_{\text{TRSP}}$state transitions per TRSP (region transition procedure)≈ 6
$t_{\text{SCP}}$state transitions per SCP (server-crash procedure)≈ 9

Derived quantities used throughout:

$$ \begin{aligned} N_{\text{dead}} &= fN \approx 167 \\ N_{\text{alive}} &= (1-f)N \approx 333 \\ M_{\text{TRSP}} &= N_{\text{dead}} \cdot R \approx 250{,}000 \quad \text{(region transitions)} \\ \lambda_{\text{RS}} &= \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}}{N_{\text{alive}}} \approx 750 \quad \text{(dispatches per surviving RS)} \end{aligned} $$

3. Recovery-Work Model

The TRSP term dominates the SCP term by three orders of magnitude, so the total work performed by the procedure executor during recovery is well approximated by the TRSP transitions alone:

$$ W_{\text{total}} \;\approx\; M_{\text{TRSP}} \cdot t_{\text{TRSP}} \;\approx\; 1.5 \times 10^{6} \;\;\text{state transitions.} $$

Sizing decisions key off the TRSP arrival rate. The SCP burst matters only as an instantaneous concurrency lower bound, picked up at L7.

4. Placement and WAL provider assumptions

The reference deployment colocates RegionServers and DataNodes one-to-one across three availability zones. WAL blocks are placed in each of the cluster's three AZs (registered as racks). The loss of one AZ therefore reduces every active write pipeline cluster wide simultaneously, and at the moment of AZ loss every surviving RegionServer's open WAL drops from a width-3 to a width-2 pipeline at once. This single placement constraint is what couples the L2 cross-section impact to every higher layer.

The WAL provider is AsyncFSWAL, selected by hbase.wal.provider = asyncfs. AsyncFSWAL's single-block-per-file design sidesteps several pipeline-recovery hazards that the legacy FSHLog provider exhibits when an AZ is lost. The cross-section derivations from L2 onward assume AsyncFSWAL throughout. The FSHLog mechanics are still documented at L2 for reference, but the recommended deployment is AsyncFSWAL.

At the reference workload, the strict placement constraint pins the cluster-wide partial-failure footprint to a small set of counts:

SymbolMeaningValue
$N_{\text{alive}}$surviving RegionServers (and DataNodes)333
$N_{\text{dead}}$DataNodes in the lost AZ$\lceil 500/3 \rceil = 167$
$N_{\text{pipelines-degraded}}$WAL pipelines reduced to width 2 at the moment of AZ loss$N_{\text{alive}} = 333$

5. Mathematical framework

Every per-stage decision in this document is one of three uses of Little's Law:

$$ L \;=\; \lambda\, W $$

where

  • $L$ = number of items concurrently in the stage (workers, handlers, threads in use),
  • $\lambda$ = arrival rate at the stage,
  • $W$ = time each item spends in the stage (service or hold time).

$L = \lambda W$ is solved for whichever variable is unknown: for thread-pool sizing $L$ is the unknown (metahandler.count, openregion.threads, procedure.threads); for the procedure-store WAL it is $\lambda$ (sustained throughput).

Recovery window estimates use a separate identity, the fluid-flow drain time:

$$ T_{\text{drain}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}}{\mu}, $$

where $W_{\text{total}}$ is the aggregate workload through the stage during recovery and $\mu$ is the stage's service rate. Throughout the document, Little's Law is used to size concurrency (the in-flight population $L$ at each stage) and the fluid-flow equation is used to bound recovery time (how long it takes a stage to drain a known workload). The two are kept notationally distinct: $L$ always denotes a concurrent population, $W$ a per-item time, and $W_{\text{total}}$ a cumulative workload.

A handful of stages are not flow-balance problems but instantaneous burst-concurrency problems. For those the rule is simply $L \geq$ (simultaneous events in the burst) + margin. Burst-concurrency arguments appear at L7 for the master executor pools.

L1 — HDFS NameNode

The NameNode is the bottom layer. Every region open issues $\omega \approx 30$ NameNode RPCs (one getFileInfo + one getBlockLocations per HFile, multiplied across HFiles per column family per region), so the NameNode's sustained throughput sets the cluster wide concurrency ceiling on region opens. The NameNode also imposes a wall clock cost on a faile active scenario.

L1.1 Model

Treat the active NameNode as a single shared resource with op-throughput ceiling $C_{\text{NN}}$. Under HA, decompose the NameNode-unavailable wall-clock into an HDFS-side bracket plus a client-side residual:

$$ T_{\text{NN-perceived}} \;\le\; \underbrace{T_{\text{NN-failover}}^{(\text{HDFS-side})}}_{\text{ZKFC + standby promotion + block-report quorum}} \;+\; \underbrace{T_{\text{client-stuck-on-hung-active}}^{(\text{client-side})}}_{\text{embedded } \texttt{DFSClient} \text{ residual}}. $$

The HDFS-side bracket is dominated by ZKFC fencing + ZK session expiry (~30 s at standard 30 s session timeout), standby promotion + edit-log catch-up (~10–20 s for a current standby), and DataNode block report quorum to the new active before safe mode exit (~60–120 s; scaling with total block count).

The recovery impact is the time the embedded DFSClient in HBase JVMs spends stuck on a hung or dead NN before RetryPolicies.failoverOnNetworkException advances the proxy to a newly active standby. This is tunable from hbase-site.xml. NameNode RPC traffic flows through a FailoverProxyProvider (typically ConfiguredFailoverProxyProvider) wrapped by RetryPolicies.failoverOnNetworkException. The FPP's internal AbstractNNFailoverProxyProvider reads dfs.client.failover.connection.retries and dfs.client.failover.connection.retries.on.timeouts and writes them back into each per-NN proxy's Configuration as ipc.client.connect.max.retries and ipc.client.connect.max.retries.on.timeouts. Both default to 0 for FPP-managed proxies, so a single connect failure to one NN immediately triggers failover to the next. The dominant factor on the client side is therefore the in-flight RPC timeout (ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms), not connect retry budgets.

L1.2 Configuration

NameNode instance and HDFS tuning

Run the active NameNode on m7g.16xlarge (64 vCPU AWS Graviton3, 256 GB DDR5-4800, 30 Gbps network, 20 Gbps EBS bandwidth). Three properties of this instance matter for region-open bursts:

  • 64 vCPUs support a 128-thread IPC handler pool (dfs.namenode.handler.count = 128) and a wide reader pool, so RPC processing parallelism scales with the concurrent open-RPC fan-in.
  • 256 GB of DDR5 hosts a ~200 GB JVM heap, enough to keep the full namespace in memory at reference scale, with headroom for off-heap structures and OS page cache.
  • DDR5-4800 memory bandwidth is what bounds the in-memory inode-tree traversal that dominates getFileInfo and getBlockLocations, the two operations that account for the $\omega \approx 30$ region-open footprint.

L1.3 Impact analysis

Sustained throughput

With the instance and tuning above, a single active NameNode sustains

$$ C_{\text{NN}} \;\approx\; 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s} \quad\text{(read-heavy, single active NameNode, tuned).} $$

Load during the partial-failure window

At the moment of AZ loss the NameNode sees a brief synchronous burst of addBlock RPCs as every surviving RegionServer rolls its WAL onto a fresh width-2 pipeline. The roll cascade completes within $T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \approx 6\,\text{s}$ (the AsyncFSWAL slow-packet ladder, derived in L2), so the cluster-wide rate peaks at $\lambda_{\text{addBlock}}^{\text{burst}} \approx N_{\text{alive}} / T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \approx 333 / 6 \approx 55$/s. Against the tuned ceiling $C_{\text{NN}} \approx 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$ this is under 0.02% utilization and disappears below the noise floor.

On a longer timescale the NameNode absorbs the consequences of $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ DataNodes ceasing to heartbeat. Each lost DN is marked dead only after dfs.namenode.heartbeat.recheck-interval. Averaged over the recheck window this is $\approx N_{\text{dead}} / 300\,\text{s} \approx 0.6$ dead-DN events per second, with bunching at the back end of the window. Even the local burst is small relative to $C_{\text{NN}}$. The dead DN scans queue blocks for re-replication, but the NameNode schedules transfer commands at no more than dfs.namenode.replication.max-streams-hard-limit per surviving DataNode, so the NameNode side scheduler load is bounded by that regardless of backlog size.

The aggregate of these additions is comfortably below $C_{\text{NN}}$ at the reference workload. The NameNode is unaffected at this layer.

Recovery-time contribution at NN saturation

The NameNode contribution to recovery time at the saturation limit is

$$ T_{\text{NN}} \;=\; \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}\,\omega}{C_{\text{NN}}} \;=\; \frac{2.5 \times 10^{5} \cdot 30}{3 \times 10^{5}} \;\approx\; 25\,\text{s.} $$

At the reference $\omega$ and $C_{\text{NN}}$ this term sits well below the master procedure store drain time derived at L6, so HDFS NameNode throughput is not the system wide bottleneck. $T_{\text{NN}}$ as derived here is the throughput bound contribution at NN saturation.

Client-side failover residual

At HDFS defaults the client-side residual can add up to ${\sim}120\,\text{s}$ to the perceived unavailability bracket (one stuck call costing one full ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms = 120\,\text{s}). At the recommended 30 second timeout this collapses to ${\le}\,30\,\text{s}$.

$$ T_{\text{NN-stuck}} \;\le\; \texttt{ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms} \;=\; 30\,\text{s.} $$

  • $C_{\text{NN}} \approx 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$ — NameNode op-throughput ceiling, consumed by L5 (region-open NN saturation match) and L8 ($T_{\text{NN}}$ term in the recovery max).

L2 — HDFS DataNodes / write pipeline

Above the NameNode sits the DataNode write pipeline, the layer that carries every WAL append, every procedure store hflush, and every recovered.edits write through three (or under AZ loss, two) replicas.

L2.1 Model

The embedded HDFS client write pipeline

Every WAL append crosses an embedded HDFS write pipeline of replication DataNodes (3 in the reference deployment). Pipeline construction is performed by the NameNode's BlockPlacementPolicy at addBlock time, honoring the client supplied excludedNodes set. Pipeline recovery is governed by a client side state machine that differs sharply between the two HBase WAL providers.

flowchart LR RS[RS WAL writer] --> DFS[DFSClient] DFS -- "addBlock(src, excludedNodes)" --> NN[("NameNode")] NN -- "LocatedBlock[DN1, DN2, DN3]" --> DFS DFS -- data --> DN1["DN1 (head)"] DN1 -- data --> DN2["DN2 (mirror)"] DN2 -- data --> DN3["DN3 (tail)"] DN3 -. ack .-> DN2 DN2 -. ack .-> DN1 DN1 -. ack .-> DFS

On FSHLog the writer is a stock DFSOutputStream. Pipeline failures trigger HDFS's pipeline recovery machinery. On AsyncFSWAL the writer is HBase's own FanOutOneBlockAsyncDFSOutput, which avoids the complexity and latency of pipeline recovery by only ever writing files the length of a single block. We assume AsyncFSWAL is configured for the production deployment.

Partial-failure detection windows

Various NameNode side mechanisms can keep a degraded DN out of new pipelines. At HDFS defaults each has a different detection window and a different set of opt-ins.

Dead-DN window. The NameNode declares a DN dead only after a long no-heartbeat window:

$$ T_{\text{dead}} \;=\; 2\,t_{\text{recheck}} \;+\; 10\,t_{\text{hb}}. $$

With defaults dfs.namenode.heartbeat.recheck-interval = 5 min and dfs.heartbeat.interval = 3 s, this is 10 min 30 s. Until the dead window expires, the DN remains IN_SERVICE and the placement policy will keep proposing it.

Stale-DN window. dfs.namenode.stale.datanode.interval (default 30 s) marks a DN stale after a missed heartbeat. Stale DNs are avoided as targets for new pipelines if dfs.namenode.avoid.write.stale.datanode is true. A safety hatch (dfs.namenode.write.stale.datanode.ratio, default 0.5) disables stale-DN avoidance when more than half the cluster is stale, so during a broad partition the protection disengages exactly when it matters most. Stale-DN avoidance only fires when a DN actually misses heartbeats. A degraded DN that continues to heartbeat normally never goes stale. The slow peer graylist is the only NN-side mechanism that catches that case.

Slow peer graylist. Each DN computes an outlier set over its downstream send latencies (DataNodePeerMetrics with OutlierDetector) and ships them in heartbeats. The NameNode aggregates votes in SlowPeerTracker, a periodic task materializes a UUID set, and BlockPlacementPolicyDefault.isGoodDatanode rejects DNs in that set during placement. Three opt-ins are required, all default false: dfs.datanode.peer.stats.enabled (DN-side reporting), dfs.namenode.block-placement-policy.exclude-slow-nodes.enabled (NN-side enforcement), and a non-zero dfs.namenode.slowpeer.collect.interval. Worst case time from when the DN begins to misbehave to when it appears in the graylist:

$$ T_{\text{graylist}} \;\lesssim\; 3\,t_{\text{outliers.report.interval}} \;+\; t_{\text{slowpeer.collect.interval}}. $$

At defaults this is ${\sim}\,$2 hours, and even then the exclude slow nodes switch controls whether the NN actually refuses the DN. Out of the box the NN sees the slow peer reports but does nothing with them.

Load based exclusion. dfs.namenode.redundancy.considerLoad (default true, factor 2.0) skips a DN whose xceiverCount exceeds the cluster average. A DN with a hot disk or facing network degradation slows packet acks without inflating xceiver counts.

FSHLog DataStreamer

For deployments that have not switched to AsyncFSWAL, the writer is a stock DFSOutputStream / DataStreamer. The client maintains a per-stream excludedNodes cache, populated whenever a pipeline DN fails and passed into the next addBlock. The replace-datanode-on-failure policy decides what to do when a DN drops mid write. The client can ask the NameNode for an additional DN via getAdditionalDatanode and grow the surviving pipeline back to a chosen replication floor, then drive a generation stamp recovery via updateBlockForPipeline. The state machine that ties these together lives in DataStreamer:

FSHLog tolerates a partial pipeline failure provided the NN can find a replacement DN. The cost is one GS bump and one extra addBlock round-trip. Otherwise FSHLog.doCheckLogLowReplication forces a roll when the current block has fewer than hbase.regionserver.hlog.tolerable.lowreplication live replicas. AsyncFSWAL fan-out and ExcludeDatanodeManager

AsyncFSWAL replaces the stock HDFS client with FanOutOneBlockAsyncDFSOutput. Each WAL file is bounded to exactly one HDFS block, sidestepping the entire chained pipeline construction and recovery machinery. HBase's custom client opens one Netty channel per replica and streams packets to all DNs in parallel. There is no head/mirror/tail relationship between the DNs.

AsyncFSWAL ignores dfs.client.block.write.replace-datanode-on-failure.*. On any DN error, all pending acks fail, and the upper layer immediately rolls the WAL. Because each WAL file is one block, this is as simple as closing the broken file, opening a fresh file with a fresh addBlock, and starting a fresh fan-out. For the roll to be productive, hbase-asyncfs carries its own degradation tracker. StreamSlowMonitor observes per-packet ack latency on each in-flight WAL writer and decides when a DN's behavior is degraded enough to act on, and ExcludeDatanodeManager holds the resulting per-RegionServer registry of DNs to avoid. AsyncFSWAL keeps writing within $T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \approx 6\,\text{s}$ of an AZ loss.

L2.3 Recommendations summary

The combined effect on $T_{\text{graylist}}$ at the recommended DataNode and NameNode settings is:

$$ T_{\text{graylist}}^{\text{(recommended)}} \;\lesssim\; 3 \cdot 60\,\text{s} \;+\; 60\,\text{s} \;=\; 4\,\text{min,} $$

compared to ${\sim}\,$2 h at defaults. $T_{\text{dead}}$ shrinks to 2 min 30 s, and the stale-DN write avoidance window continues to provide a 30 s safety valve for DNs that miss heartbeats. With AsyncFSWAL throughout, the RS-level circuit breakers and exclude-list cache (sized at L4) cap detection-to-roll latency well below those NN-side windows.

L2.4 Impact analysis: cross-section under one-replica-per-AZ placement

At the moment one of three AZs is lost, $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ DataNodes vanish, and every active WAL pipeline cluster-wide instantly drops one replica. Because each pipeline started at width $r = 3$ with one replica per AZ, $N_{\text{pipelines-degraded}} = N_{\text{alive}} = 333$ — every surviving RegionServer's open WAL is affected simultaneously. AsyncFSWAL's StreamSlowMonitor sees the IO failure on the lost-AZ replica, the upper layer rolls onto a fresh width-2 file via addBlock, and the surviving cluster reaches a steady state of width-2 WAL writers within seconds of the AZ loss.

WAL-roll burst at AZ loss

Time-to-detect the lost AZ replica is bounded by three consecutive packets at the slow packet threshold trip StreamSlowMonitor, after which the upper layer rolls on the next sync. At the recommended async.wal.datanode.slow.packet.process.time.millis = 2000 and async.wal.min.slow.detect.count = 3:

$$ T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \;\approx\; N_{\text{min}} \cdot T_{\text{slow-packet}} \;=\; 3 \cdot 2\,\text{s} \;=\; 6\,\text{s.} $$

$N_{\text{pipelines-degraded}} = 333$ surviving RegionServers each issue one addBlock over that interval:

$$ \lambda_{\text{addBlock}}^{\text{burst}} \;\approx\; \frac{N_{\text{alive}}}{T_{\text{roll-trigger}}} \;=\; \frac{333}{6\,\text{s}} \;\approx\; 55\,\text{addBlock/s.} $$

Negligible against $C_{\text{NN}} \approx 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$. L1 already absorbed this term.

Bounded RS abort count

A surviving RegionServer aborts when an in-flight WAL sync fails to acknowledge within hbase.regionserver.wal.sync.timeout (sized at L4). With AsyncFSWAL the bad replica error surfaces within $T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \approx 6\,\text{s}$ and the writer rolls onto a healthy width-2 pipeline, well below the recommended wal.sync.timeout of 60 s, so the watchdog ordinarily never fires. An abort therefore requires a residual case where slow tail conditions persist through the roll. Bounding by the steady state slow sync tail probability $P_{\text{slow-sync}} \lesssim 0.01$:

$$ N_{\text{abort}} \;\lesssim\; N_{\text{alive}} \cdot P_{\text{slow-sync}} \;\approx\; 333 \cdot 0.01 \;\lesssim\; 3. $$

HDFS-side load delta during the recovery window

The cluster wide write rate from RegionServer WAL traffic is unchanged after AZ loss. The same offered $\lambda_{\text{wal}}$ now writes two replicas instead of three, and 333 surviving DataNodes carry it instead of 500. The per-DataNode write rate stays at $\lambda_{\text{wal}} \cdot 3 / 500 = \lambda_{\text{wal}} \cdot 2 / 333$. Per-DataNode read rate is a different story. HFile and recovered-edit reads were striped across three replicas with locality preference, and now stripe across two with the same preference. The per-DN read rate climbs:

$$ \Delta \lambda_{\text{DN-read}} \;\approx\; \frac{r}{r-1} - 1 \;=\; \frac{3}{2} - 1 \;=\; 0.5 \quad (+50\%). $$

L3 — HDFS DataNodes / read path

L2 covers the writer pipeline path. The read path has its own set of detection windows and timeouts. At HDFS defaults each region open and each WAL split is exposed to a worst case 60 second per-block stall when an embedded DFSClient picks a dead replica. This layer's job is to bound that residual via hedged reads, a reduced socket timeout, and a JVM-shared DeadNodeDetector.

L3.1 Model

Tail-risk decomposition

A region open or WAL split that picks an unhealthy replica can stall in three distinct ways:

  • Hung-DN-before-timeout. A lost-AZ DataNode reachable enough to complete a TCP handshake but unresponsive to the block-read RPC. The client waits up to dfs.client.socket-timeout + READ_TIMEOUT_EXTENSION $\approx 65\,\text{s}$ at HDFS defaults before retrying on a different replica.
  • Slow-but-alive DN. A surviving DataNode whose per-block read latency has slipped into the queueing-stretched tail (P99 in the hundreds of milliseconds to several seconds once the +50% load from L2 tips the DN toward saturation).
  • Black-holed NN traffic. Treated at L1, not here.

Hedged reads

DFSInputStream.hedgedFetchBlockByteRange issues the primary block read on the chosen replica, waits up to dfs.client.hedged.read.threshold.millis ($\theta$), and on threshold expiry submits a parallel read against another replica via the JVM-wide HEDGED_READ_THREAD_POOL. Whichever read completes first wins. The losers are cancelled. The pool size is gated by dfs.client.hedged.read.threadpool.size. At the default of 0 the path is disabled entirely.

sequenceDiagram participant C as DFSClient participant P as Primary DN participant A as Alt DN C->>P: chooseDataNode + block read (X1) Note right of C: wait up to θ alt X1 < θ P-->>C: data (winner) else X1 ≥ θ C->>A: hedged read on alt replica (X2) Note over P,A: race, first to complete wins A-->>C: data (if A wins, cancel P) P-->>C: data (if P wins after θ, cancel A) end

Effective wait under hedging, with $F$ the per-replica latency CDF, $\theta$ the threshold, $X_1$ primary latency, and $X_2$ alt-replica latency:

$$ E[\text{wait}_{\text{hedged}}] \;=\; E[X_1 \mid X_1 < \theta]\cdot F(\theta) \;+\; E[\min(X_1,\;\theta + X_2) \mid X_1 \ge \theta]\cdot \big(1 - F(\theta)\big). $$

The threshold has to sit above the healthy P99 so it does not fire in steady state and below the slow tail values that the recovery window introduces. The per-replica fire rate is $1 - F(\theta)$. Each fire issues one extra read on an alternate replica, so the hedged read load amplification on the cross-section of surviving DNs is the same fraction.

In steady state, healthy P99 block-read latency on DataNodes sits at ${\sim}\,50\,\text{ms}$, comfortably below $\theta = 100\,\text{ms}$. The hedge-fire rate is $1 - F_{\text{ss}}(100\,\text{ms}) \approx 0.01$, so hedged reads add ${\lesssim}\,1\%$ to per-DN read load.

In the AZ-loss window, per-DN read load rises $\approx 50\%$ (L2), pushing per-DN utilization from $\rho \approx 0.5$ toward $\rho \approx 0.75$ on the surviving DataNodes. Under an M/M/1 approximation the latency tail stretches by $(1-\rho_{\text{ss}})/(1-\rho_{\text{AZ}}) = 0.5/0.25 = 2$, so $P_{99}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 100\text{--}125\,\text{ms}$. The hedge fire rate climbs to $1 - F_{\text{AZ}}(100\,\text{ms}) \approx 0.05\text{--}0.10$, and the per-DN read load envelope settles near

$$ \lambda_{\text{DN-read}}^{\text{AZ, hedged}} \;\approx\; 1.5\,\lambda_0 \cdot \big(1 + 0.05\text{--}0.10\big) \;\approx\; 1.58\text{--}1.65\,\lambda_0. $$

This sits comfortably below DataNode saturation in the reference deployment, so $\theta = 100\,\text{ms}$ is the right balance.

Per-block-read socket timeout

The client–DataNode block-transfer SO_TIMEOUT is dfs.client.socket-timeout (default 60000 ms, sourced from HdfsConstants.READ_TIMEOUT). The effective wait is composed with a per-pipeline-target additive in DFSClient.getDatanodeReadTimeout(numNodes):

$$ T_{\text{block-read}} \;=\; \texttt{dfs.client.socket-timeout} \;+\; \texttt{READ\_TIMEOUT\_EXTENSION} \cdot N, $$

with READ_TIMEOUT_EXTENSION = 5000 ms (a compile-time constant) and $N$ the number of DataNodes the call traverses (1 for a block read). At defaults this caps a hung-DN read at $\approx 65\,\text{s}$ before the client gives up. With hedged reads enabled this floor only matters when both replicas time out simultaneously; otherwise hedging short-circuits the wait at $\theta + p_{50,\text{alt}} \approx 150\,\text{ms}$. Reducing the floor to 15000 ms shortens the residual worst case by $4\times$ while leaving generous headroom above the recovery-window P99 of ${\sim}\,125\,\text{ms}$.

Cross-stream DeadNodeDetector

At HDFS defaults, dead DN tracking is per-DFSInputStream, built on read failure and discarded when the stream closes. Under AZ loss this means each fresh DFSInputStream rediscovers the same set of dead DataNodes independently, paying the dfs.client.socket-timeout stall once per stream per dead DN it happens to pick.

HDFS-13571 / HDFS-14542 introduced an opt-in DeadNodeDetector daemon attached to ClientContext, the per-JVM cache that holds the peer cache, short-circuit fd cache, and located blocks refresher. With dfs.client.deadnode.detection.enabled = true and a single dfs.client.context name across all DFSClient instances in the JVM, the dead node set is populated exactly once on first failure and reused for every subsequent DFSInputStream. The detector probes asynchronously on its own pools and removes a DN from the dead set when a probe succeeds.

L3.3 Impact analysis: per-RS amortized seeding cost

Without the cross-stream DeadNodeDetector, each DFSInputStream rediscovers dead DNs independently. With $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ and a per-block-read first-pick-dead probability $p_{\text{first-pick-dead}} \approx N_{\text{dead}}/N_{\text{total}} = 0.33$, the per-stream expected stall count is $0.33 \cdot k_{\text{reads/open}}$, each costing up to dfs.client.socket-timeout. That cost multiplies across the $M_{\text{TRSP}} = 250{,}000$ region opens during recovery, with no amortization across streams.

With DeadNodeDetector enabled and dfs.client.context pinned to a single name, the dead-node set is per-RS-process. Once the first stream in the JVM observes a failure on a dead DN, every subsequent stream skips that DN for free. Composition with the L2 mean-latency multipliers

The two analyses compose without double counting. The mean latency component of L2 — per-DN read latency rising $\approx 50\%$ during recovery and inflating $\bar{h}^{\text{AZ}}$, $W_{\text{open}}^{\text{AZ}}$, and $T_{\text{WAL}}^{\text{AZ}}$ each by $\approx 1.5\times$ — is unchanged by the read-path tuning here, since none of these parameters alter the aggregate read work the surviving DataNodes carry. The worst case per-event stall residual shrinks from ${\sim}\,65\,\text{s}$ (socket-timeout bounded) to ${\sim}\,150\,\text{ms}$ on the read path. The L1 tuning analogously collapses the NN-side per-event residual from ${\sim}\,120\,\text{s}$ to ${\sim}\,30\,\text{s}$. The L8 composition rule combines the two without double counting.

L4 — RegionServer WAL writer

Above the HDFS layers sits the RegionServer WAL writer. Its job in the recovery model is to react quickly when one of the DNs in a write pipeline is degraded, fast enough that L2's width-2 steady state actually holds and the watchdog at hbase.regionserver.wal.sync.timeout never fires unless the pipeline is genuinely unrecoverable.

AsyncFSWAL's StreamSlowMonitor observes per-packet ack latency on each in-flight WAL writer. The slow packet decision in StreamSlowMonitor.checkProcessTimeAndSpeed uses two thresholds keyed off packet size, on the principle that small packets are bounded by per-RPC latency while large packets are bounded by throughput. Packets below hbase.regionserver.async.wal.datanode.slow.check.speed.packet.data.length.min (default 64 KiB) are judged against hbase.regionserver.async.wal.datanode.slow.packet.process.time.millis (default 6 000 ms). Larger packets are judged against hbase.regionserver.async.wal.datanode.slow.packet.speed.min.kbs (default 20 KB/s). Either threshold being crossed contributes one observation against the DN.

Observations are accumulated per DN in a sliding deque whose validity window is hbase.regionserver.async.wal.slow.detect.data.ttl.ms (default 10 min), and only when a DN accumulates hbase.regionserver.async.wal.min.slow.detect.count (default 3) live observations is it excluded.

ExcludeDatanodeManager holds at most hbase.regionserver.async.wal.max.exclude.datanode.count (default 3) DataNodes, each retained for hbase.regionserver.async.wal.exclude.datanode.info.ttl.hour (default 6 h). On each subsequent WAL roll the current registry contents are passed as the excludedNodes argument to the NameNode's addBlock call, so the placement policy is barred from returning those DNs in the new file's pipeline.

Because ExcludeDatanodeManager is instantiated per-RegionServer process, a bad DN is rediscovered independently by each surviving RegionServer once the slow-packet quorum has accumulated against that DN from that RS's own WAL traffic. Cluster wide convergence takes as many independent detections as there are RegionServers writing through the bad DN. The NameNode graylist (sized at L2) is the only mechanism with true cluster wide scope.

The default cap of 3 cannot represent a large blast-radius incident. In an AZ loss with $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ DataNodes simultaneously degraded, the cap must be raised so the registry can hold the full set of concurrently degraded DNs without losing coverage to thrashing.

L5 — RegionServer execution (split + open)

This is the layer that actually does the recovery work on the RegionServer side, splitting the WALs of dead RegionServers into recovered.edits and opening the regions of those dead RSs on surviving hosts. Both stages consume HDFS service times that L2's mean latency multipliers and L3's tail risk bound combine to make finite.

L5.1 Distributed WAL splitting

Model

Each dead RS leaves an unarchived WAL set that the master must split before its regions can be reassigned. Per dead RS, the master enumerates the files in WALs/<server>-splitting/, creates one SplitWALProcedure per WAL, and dispatches each as a child SplitWALRemoteProcedure to a worker chosen by the WorkerAssigner. The worker runs the split on its RS_LOG_REPLAY_OPS executor: a sequential HDFS read of the WAL plus triple-replicated writes of the per-region recovered.edits files. The master polls for the source WAL no longer existing at its splitting-dir path before dispatching the corresponding TRSPs.

$$ P_{\text{split}} \;=\; N_{\text{alive}} \cdot K, \qquad K \,=\, \texttt{hbase.regionserver.wal.max.splitters}. $$

These quantities define the WAL workload:

  • $N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ — unarchived WAL files per dead RS at outage time. Workload-dependent, capped above by hbase.regionserver.maxlogs (default $\geq 32$).
  • $M_{\text{wal}} = N_{\text{dead}} \cdot N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ — total WAL files to split. At the reference workload, $M_{\text{wal}}$ ranges from $\approx 1{,}300$ (typical write load, $N_{\text{wal/RS}} \approx 8$) to $\approx 5{,}300$ (write-heavy, $N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ approaching maxlogs).
  • $W_{\text{split}}$ — per-WAL split service time, HDFS-bound by a sequential read of $S$ bytes plus 3-replicated recovered.edits writes of up to $S$ bytes, with $S$ bounded above by logrollsize (default $\approx 128\,\text{MiB}$). Empirically $\approx 3\,\text{s}$ with healthy DataNodes, rising to $\approx 15\,\text{s}$ under DataNode contention. The +50% read-load delta from L2 contributes a $1.5\times$ multiplier on the mean; the $T_{\text{ghost-stall}}$ bound from L3 caps the tail.

Applying Little's Law to the split-slot pool, $M_{\text{wal}}$ items of mean service $W_{\text{split}}$ through $P_{\text{split}}$ servers, and the WAL-splitting drain time is:

$$ T_{\text{WAL}} \;=\; \frac{M_{\text{wal}} \cdot W_{\text{split}}}{P_{\text{split}}} \;=\; \frac{N_{\text{dead}} \cdot N_{\text{wal/RS}} \cdot W_{\text{split}}}{N_{\text{alive}} \cdot K}. $$

L5.2 Region open executor

Model

Each surviving RegionServer maintains its own pool to execute RS_OPEN_REGION events (and a parallel pool for RS_CLOSE_REGION). The default is 3. During AZ-loss recovery, each surviving RS receives ≈ 750 OPEN dispatches, and the work each executor thread does for one open is dominated by HDFS reads, not CPU.

Per-RS open throughput:

$$ \mu_{\text{open}}^{\text{(per RS)}} \;=\; \frac{T_{\text{open}}}{W_{\text{open}}} \quad\text{(regions/sec/RS),} $$

where $T_{\text{open}}$ is the configured thread count and $W_{\text{open}}$ is the per-region open time. Cluster-wide:

L6 — Master procedure store

The procedure store is the durable substrate behind every state transition in HBase recovery. It is a single-writer log (a MasterRegion with an AsyncFSWAL) and so its throughput is governed by how many threads are blocked on persist, and how long each persist takes. This layer turns that into a sustained throughput target and a drain time bound, then feeds the result up to L7, which sizes the surrounding executors to feed the sink, and L8, which composes the drain time into the recovery max.

L6.1 Model

Closed-loop throughput

With $N$ procedure threads, $w$ = sync-wait, and $h$ = hflush latency under stress, the closed loop, work conserving throughput is:

$$ \mu_{\text{proc}} \;\approx\; \frac{N}{w + h} \quad\text{(writes/second).} $$

The drain time for the recovery work follows directly:

$$ T_{\text{drain}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}}{\mu_{\text{proc}}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}\,(w + h)}{N}. $$

At the HBase defaults ($N = 16$, $w = 100\,\text{ms}$, $h = 10\,\text{ms}$), $\mu_{\text{proc}} \approx 145\,\text{w/s}$, orders of magnitude below the offered rate. The deterministic formula is enough to confirm that $N$ and $w$ must change by orders of magnitude. The actual operating point is set by the bulk-service queueing analysis below, which adds service-time variance to the picture.

Bulk-service queueing

The closed loop throughput formula treats service time as deterministic, which is sufficient to size $\mu$ but not to size headroom. The procedure store hflush is not deterministic: $p_{50} \approx 7\,\text{ms}$, $p_{99} \gtrsim 25\,\text{ms}$ under stress. Since each flush cycle serves up to $N$ writes in bulk, the right model is an $M/G^{[N]}/1$ bulk-service queue, and the design rule must guard against per-cycle arrival overspill rather than per-write wait.

The cycle level service time setup is:

  • Service time per cycle: $S = w + h$, where $w$ is the dwell wait (deterministic) and $h$ is the hflush latency (random).
  • $E[S] = w + \bar{h}$, $\;\text{Var}(S) = \sigma_h^{2}$, $\;C_S^{2} = \sigma_h^{2}/(w+\bar{h})^{2}$.
  • Each cycle serves up to $N$ procedure writes. Cluster throughput: $\mu = N / E[S]$. Per-write utilization: $\rho = \lambda E[S] / N$.

For $w = 10\,\text{ms}$, $\bar{h} = 10\,\text{ms}$, $\sigma_h \approx 8\,\text{ms}$ (consistent with $p_{99} \approx 30\,\text{ms}$):

$$ E[S] = 20\,\text{ms},\quad C_S^{2} = \frac{64}{400} = 0.16. $$

Design rule: bulk-cycle variance bound

In a bulk queue, multi-cycle backlog only occurs if the number of arrivals during a single flush cycle exceeds $N$, causing writes to spill over into the next cycle. The relevant tail to bound is therefore the per-cycle arrival overflow probability, not the per-write residual wait.

For a cycle of random length $S = w + h$, the number of Poisson arrivals $A$ during the cycle satisfies $\mu_A = \lambda E[S] = \rho N$ and $\sigma_A^{2} = \rho N + \rho^{2} N^{2} C_S^{2}$ (Poisson term plus the law of total variance contribution from random $S$). Adopt the design rule that capacity $N$ must cover the mean plus a $1.33\sigma$ safety margin (so $\approx 90\%$ of cycles clear completely without spillover, even during slow flushes):

$$ N \;\geq\; \mu_A + 1.33\,\sigma_A. $$

Substituting $N = 512$ and $C_S^{2} = 0.16$, evaluate at a target utilization of $\rho = 0.65$:

$$ \begin{aligned} \mu_A &= 0.65 \cdot 512 \;\approx\; 333, \\ \sigma_A^{2} &= 333 + (0.65)^{2}(512)^{2}(0.16) \;\approx\; 18{,}054 \;\Longrightarrow\; \sigma_A \;\approx\; 134, \\ \mu_A + 1.33\,\sigma_A &= 333 + 1.33 \cdot 134 \;\approx\; 511.2 \;\leq\; 512. \end{aligned} $$

This pins the maximum sustainable steady-state utilization to $\rho_{\max} \approx 0.65$. Operating above this threshold sharply increases the probability of multi-cycle overspill. As $\rho \to 1$ the arrival mean approaches $N$ while $\sigma_A$ continues to grow, so an increasing fraction of cycles spill into the next, and backlog accumulates. The same $\rho_{\max} \approx 0.65$ engineering target is also the right margin to absorb physical jitter (JVM GC pauses, transient DataNode pipeline stalls) that inflate $\sigma_h$ above its nominal value.

L7 — Master orchestration

With the L6 procedure store sized to sustain $\mu_{\text{proc}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 20.5\,\text{K w/s}$, the remaining master-side question is whether the surrounding executors can keep that sink fed without starvation. The answer is given by five sub-pools: SCP burst absorption, remote dispatch, master-side OPEN execution against META, master-side RPC handlers, and the dispatcher dwell timer. Each is sized either by a burst-concurrency lower bound (one-shot fan-outs) or by Little's Law against a downstream rate.

L7.1 SCP burst: hbase.master.executor.serverops.threads

The serverops executor handles inbound server-lifecycle events. At AZ loss, $\approx 167$ dead-server notifications fire near-simultaneously and each must dispatch into an SCP without queuing. This is not a flow problem; it is a one-shot burst:

$$ L_{\text{serverops}} \;\geq\; N_{\text{dead}} \;\approx\; 167 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 256 \;\text{(next power of two, with margin).} $$

At the default of 5, the burst is processed effectively serially.

L7.2 Remote dispatcher: hbase.procedure.remote.dispatcher.threadpool.size

The remote-dispatcher pool runs the per-RS dispatch loops that flush batched RPCs. Concurrency must cover all destinations simultaneously:

$$ L_{\text{disp}} \;\geq\; N_{\text{alive}} \;\approx\; 333 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 512 \;\text{(headroom for retries on transient failures).} $$

Below ${\sim}\,333$ the dispatcher itself becomes a bottleneck independent of any other tuning.

L7.3 Dispatcher dwell tax: hbase.procedure.remote.dispatcher.delay.msec

The dispatcher's dwell timer $d$ is the time it waits to accumulate per-RS RPCs into a single batched flush. During recovery, each remote callable's completion is a serialization point for the next procedure transition, so the dwell tax is paid per transition:

$$ \Delta_{\text{dwell, worst-case}} \;=\; d \cdot t_{\text{TRSP}}. $$

Above ${\sim}\,100$ ms the cumulative dispatcher dwell becomes a measurable contribution to recovery latency on its own.

L7.4 Master openregion executor: hbase.master.executor.openregion.threads

This executor processes reportRegionStateTransition OPEN events. The downstream constraint is the META write rate. With a sustainable META write rate $\lambda_{\text{META}} \approx 5{,}000\,\text{w/s}$ and per-handler hold time $W_{\text{handler}} \approx 50\,\text{ms}$:

$$ L_{\text{open}} \;=\; \lambda_{\text{META}} \cdot W_{\text{handler}} \;=\; 5{,}000 \cdot 0.050 \;\approx\; 250. $$

Anything less and META is left underutilized. The recommended 256 sits one power of two above this. The close-region executor follows symmetrically.

L7.5 Master metahandler: hbase.regionserver.metahandler.count

All inbound traffic from RegionServers during recovery is tagged HIGH_QOS by MasterAnnotationReadingPriorityFunction and lands in the master's priority handler pool. Handlers hold for a procedure store hflush plus region lock acquisition (≈ 25 ms typical). Arrival rate during the steepest portion of the recovery curve is ≈ 5–10K events/sec:

$$ L_{\text{rpc}} \;=\; \lambda_{\text{rpc}} \cdot W_{\text{hold}} \;\approx\; (5\text{–}10) \times 10^{3}\,\text{s}^{-1} \times 0.025\,\text{s} \;\approx\; 125\text{–}250. $$

The recommended 256 covers the upper end with margin.

L7.6 Offered vs. sustained load

The instantaneous offered rate during the SCP fan-out is:

$$ \lambda_{\text{offered}} \;\approx\; \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}}{T_{\text{create}}} \;=\; \frac{2.5 \times 10^{5}}{5\text{–}10\,\text{s}} \;=\; 25\text{–}50\,\text{K/s.} $$

At the upper end, this exceeds the recommended sustained throughput $\mu_{\text{proc}} \approx 25.6\,\text{K/s}$ from L6. The offered burst exceeds the sink for ${\sim}\,5\text{--}10$ s and the resulting backlog drains over the remainder of the recovery window. The design constraint is that the sink drains $W_{\text{total}}$ within the chosen recovery window, which the L6 sizing already meets.

L8 — Recovery time composition

Recovery time is the max of per-stage drain times plus a small pipeline overhead. The model gives a closed-form bound at the reference workload ($N=500$, $R=1{,}500$, $f=\tfrac{1}{3}$, $M_{\text{TRSP}}=250{,}000$, $W_{\text{total}}=1.5\times 10^{6}$). At the recommended settings of L1–L7, four stage rates set the operating bound:

$$ \begin{aligned} T_{\text{proc}} &= \frac{W_{\text{total}}(w+h)}{N_{\text{proc}}} = \frac{1.5\times 10^{6}\cdot 0.020}{512} \;\approx\; 59\,\text{s,} \\[2pt] T_{\text{WAL}} &= \frac{N_{\text{dead}}\, N_{\text{wal/RS}}\, W_{\text{split}}}{N_{\text{alive}}\, K} = \frac{167\, N_{\text{wal/RS}}\, W_{\text{split}}}{333 \cdot 8} \;\approx\; 0.063\,N_{\text{wal/RS}}\, W_{\text{split}}\,\text{s,} \\[2pt] T_{\text{RS-open}} &= \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}\,W_{\text{open}}}{N_{\text{alive}}\,T_{\text{open}}} = \frac{250{,}000\,W_{\text{open}}}{333\cdot 32} \;\approx\; 23.5\,W_{\text{open}}\,\text{s,} \\[2pt] T_{\text{NN}} &= \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}\,\omega}{C_{\text{NN}}}. \end{aligned} $$

$T_{\text{proc}}$ is fixed by tuning. $T_{\text{WAL}}$ depends on the workload driven WAL count per dead RS and the HDFS-bound per-WAL service time. $T_{\text{RS-open}}$ and $T_{\text{NN}}$ are both functions of the HDFS operating point. WAL splitting and region opens overlap at the cluster level (see L5), so $T_{\text{WAL}}$ enters the recovery max as a parallel stage rather than additively. With a small allowance $T_{\text{pipe}} \approx 10\,\text{s}$ for the SCP fan-out burst:

$$ T_{\text{recovery}} \;\approx\; \max\!\big(T_{\text{proc}},\,T_{\text{WAL}},\,T_{\text{RS-open}},\,T_{\text{NN}}\big) \;+\; T_{\text{pipe}}. $$

L8.2 Composition rule: mean-latency × tail-risk without double counting

The two HDFS-side analyses compose without double counting:

  • The mean latency component from L2 — per-DN read latency rising $\approx 50\%$ during recovery and inflating $\bar{h}^{\text{AZ}}$, $W_{\text{open}}^{\text{AZ}}$, and $T_{\text{WAL}}^{\text{AZ}}$ each by $\approx 1.5\times$ — is a queueing-load argument and is independent of how individual readers handle the resulting tail. It is unchanged by the read-path tuning of L3 and the NN-RPC tuning of L1, since neither alters the aggregate read work the surviving DataNodes carry.
  • The tail-risk component from L3 and L1. Worst case per-event time that DFSClient spends on a dead replica or a hung or dead NameNode shrinks from ${\sim}\,65\,\text{s}$ (socket-timeout bounded) or ${\sim}\,120\,\text{s}$ at HDFS defaults to ${\sim}\,150\,\text{ms}$ on the read path and ${\sim}\,30\,\text{s}$ on the NN path under recommended tuning. The per-RS amortized cost falls from ${\sim}\,5.6\,\text{min}$ at HDFS defaults to ${\sim}\,1\,\text{s}$.

L8.3 With NameNode failover

The scenarios above assume the active HDFS NameNode survives the AZ outage. If the active was in the failed AZ, HA failover to the standby in a surviving AZ sits on the critical path before HBase recovery can make meaningful progress, because region opens depend on getFileInfo/getBlockLocations from a healthy NameNode. The HDFS side bracket from L1 is

$$ T_{\text{NN-failover}} \;\approx\; 90\text{--}180\,\text{s,} $$

dominated by ZKFC fencing + ZK session expiry, standby promotion + edit-log catch-up, and DataNode block-report quorum. When the NameNode is also lost, the complete recovery wall clock time for the typical case is therefore

$$ T_{\text{recovery}}^{\text{(typical, with NN failover)}} \;\approx\; T_{\text{NN-failover}} + T_{\text{recovery}}^{\text{(typical)}} \;\approx\; \mathbf{2.5\text{--}4.5\,\text{min.}} $$

$T_{\text{NN-failover}}$ is the HDFS side wall clock time. The HBase side impact includes the time that the embedded DFSClient spends stuck on the dead NN before RetryPolicies.failoverOnNetworkException advances the proxy. At HDFS defaults this adds up to $120\,\text{s}$ per stuck call. With the recommended L1 tuning ($30\,\text{s}$ RPC timeout, $5\,\text{s}$ connect timeout) the time shrinks to $T_{\text{NN-stuck}} \le 30\,\text{s}$.

L8.6 Unifying picture

Recovery is a multi-stage pipeline that crosses three domains — master, RegionServer, and HDFS:

flowchart LR subgraph MA[Master: SCP fan-out] direction TB SCP[SCP burst] --> SO[serverops exec] SO --> PT[procedure threads] PT --> PS[(proc-store WAL)] PT --> RD[remote dispatcher] end RD -->|OPEN RPC| RS1 subgraph RS[RegionServer] direction TB RS1[handler.count] --> OE[openregion exec] end OE --> NN[(NameNode)] OE --> DN[(DataNodes)] NN --> RS2 DN --> RS2 subgraph RSC[RegionServer reportRST] RS2[reportRegionStateTransition] end RS2 -->|OPEN ack| MH subgraph MB[Master: callback] direction TB MH[metahandler] --> OE2[openregion exec] OE2 --> META[(META writes)] MH --> PT2[procedure threads] PT2 --> PS2[(proc-store WAL callback)] end

The design rule for every stage $i$ is $\mu_i \geq \lambda$, where $\mu_i$ is the per-stage service rate from Little's Law (or a burst-concurrency lower bound for the SCP burst and dispatcher pool, or a variance-bounded service rate for the procedure store). Total recovery latency is governed by the drain time of the slowest stage:

$$ T_{\text{recovery}} \;=\; \max_i \frac{W_{\text{total},i}}{\mu_i}, $$

where $W_{\text{total},i}$ is the aggregate workload routed through stage $i$ during the recovery window. This is the fluid-flow drain time, distinct from the Little's Law concurrency identity $L = \lambda W$ used in §5 to size individual thread pools. Minimizing $T_{\text{recovery}}$ requires balancing service rates across stages, not maximizing any single one. The eight layers must be tuned together.

During the AZ-loss window each stage's $\mu_i$ is the derated value from L2's mean-latency multipliers, with L3 and L1 bounding the per-event tail residuals so that no stage's drain time spikes above its mean latency value. At the reference workload the procedure store remains the bottleneck, with $T_{\text{proc-drain}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 73\,\text{s}$.

Limitations of the model

The procedure store sizing accounts for service time variance via a bulk service queueing argument (L6). The remaining first order simplifications:

HDFS treated as a single ops/sec ceiling

The NameNode is modeled as $C_{\text{NN}}$ ops/sec with no internal structure. Real NameNode behavior includes a global lock that distinguishes read paths from write paths. Under read-heavy bursts (region opens) the NN can sustain higher op rates than under mixed load. DataNode disk-read concurrency is also not modeled; HFile footer/index/bloom reads compete with normal foreground reads on the same DNs.

RS-side $W_{\text{open}}$ is a free parameter

Per-region open time is treated as an exogenous input ranging from 200 ms (healthy) to 5 s (stressed), but it is in fact a function of the number of HFiles per region, the number of recovered edits, NameNode latency, and DataNode block-cache state. A more complete model would derive $W_{\text{open}}$ from these inputs rather than parameterize it.

No contention modeling

Region locks, META row locks, and the procedure store single-writer all serialize work. The model treats each as an ideal single server queue. Lock contention between concurrent TRSPs sharing a parent SCP is not represented.

No retry amplification

Dispatcher retries on transient failures and RS-side RegionAlreadyInTransitionException retries are absorbed into "headroom" rather than modeled as $\lambda_{\text{eff}} = \lambda(1 + r)$ for a per-RPC retry rate $r$.

No coupling between META writes and the procedure WAL

Both are single writer log-structured stores. In reality their hflush streams compete for the same NameNode and DataNode pipeline capacity but the model treats them as independent.

No feedback from queue depth back to arrival

Heartbeats from surviving RSs are treated as an exogenous arrival stream. In reality, master overload increases heartbeat-handling latency, which can feed back into RS-side timeouts and retries.

Recovered edits replay not modeled separately

Under AZ loss, regions inherit recovered edits from split WALs that were owned by the dead RSs. Replay during region open is its own HDFS-bound and CPU-bound workload that the per-region open time abstraction $W_{\text{open}}$ collapses into a single number.

AsyncFSWAL is assumed throughout

The HDFS-side and cross-section derivations from §4 onward assume the AsyncFSWAL provider (hbase.wal.provider = asyncfs). L2 documents the FSHLog mechanics for reference but does not extend the model to that case.

Stale DN write avoidance and slow peer graylist limits

The stale DN write avoidance disengages when more than dfs.namenode.write.stale.datanode.ratio (default 0.5) of DNs is stale, and the slow peer graylist cannot grow beyond dfs.namenode.max.slowpeer.collect.nodes. During an AZ loss event the partial-failure protections are partially disabled exactly when the failure footprint is largest. L4's ExcludeDatanodeManager sizing recommendation is the HBase side hedge against this.

Future work

Core/overflow dynamic thread-pool sizing

The static thread counts in L6 and L7 are sized to absorb the worst-case AZ-loss burst, but the master pays for that capacity in stack memory, scheduler bookkeeping, and JVM working-set pressure proportional to the static cap. A core/overflow pool model would let the cluster run at a lower idle thread footprint while still hitting the same recovery bounds during a storm. Under this model the static values become the upper bound of the overflow tier rather than the always-on thread count. The procedure executor pool is the highest-leverage candidate ($N_{\text{proc}} = 512$ at static sizing). The burst-driven master executors (serverops, openregion, closeregion, metahandler) are next, since their utilization outside a recovery storm is near zero.

Procedure-store / META coupling

Both the procedure store WAL and the META region are single-writer log-structured stores whose hflush streams compete for the same NameNode and DataNode pipeline capacity (Limitations). Folding the coupling in would let the procedure store / META interaction on $\bar{h}$ become a first-class prediction rather than an independence assumption.

Derive $W_{\text{open}}$ from layer inputs

$W_{\text{open}}$ is currently parameterized as a scenario input at L5. A full derivation would compose the L1 NameNode op count $\omega$, the L2 mean-latency multiplier $1.5\times$, the L3 ghost-stall residual, and the per-region HFile footer/index/bloom-read pattern into a predicted $W_{\text{open}}$ for each scenario.
