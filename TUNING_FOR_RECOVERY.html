1. Abstract

This document captures a capacity model for tuning the HBase master to absorb a sudden loss of an availability zone. The reference workload is a 500-node cluster with 1,500 regions per RegionServer, suffering simultaneous loss of one third of its RegionServers (≈167 dead, ≈333 surviving), generating ≈250,000 region transitions and ≈1.5 million procedure state transitions over the recovery window.

The model is fundamentally Little's Law, $L = \lambda W$ applied independently at each stage of the recovery pipeline, together with a small number of burst-concurrency lower bounds, one closed-loop throughput equation for the procedure store, and a bulk-service queueing analysis that bounds utilization on the procedure store under realistic service-time variance. Each tuning parameter is sized so that no single stage becomes the bottleneck at the target arrival rate.

The pipeline extends past the master. A procedure state transition is not CPU bound. Each TRSP yields on a remote RPC wait while a RegionServer opens or closes the region, and that RS side work is in turn gated by HDFS NameNode and DataNode latencies for opening store files (and, under AZ loss, replaying recovered edits).

The model further assumes WAL blocks are placed with one replica per AZ. Under that constraint the document recommends AsyncWAL as the WAL provider and assumes it throughout. The HDFS dependencies are stated up front in §4; the full pipeline derivation is in §9.

2. Workload Model

Define the cluster topology and AZ-loss inputs:

SymbolMeaningValue

$t_{\text{SCP}}$state transitions per SCP (server-crash procedure)≈ 9

Derived quantities:

$$ \begin{aligned} N_{\text{dead}} &= fN \approx 167 \\ N_{\text{alive}} &= (1-f)N \approx 333 \\ M_{\text{TRSP}} &= N_{\text{dead}} \cdot R \approx 250{,}000 \quad \text{(region transitions)} \\ \lambda_{\text{RS}} &= \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}}{N_{\text{alive}}} \approx 750 \quad \text{(dispatches per surviving RS)} \end{aligned} $$

3. Recovery-Work Model

The TRSP term dominates the SCP term by three orders of magnitude, so the total work performed by the procedure executor during recovery is well approximated by the TRSP transitions alone:

$$ W_{\text{total}} \;\approx\; M_{\text{TRSP}} \cdot t_{\text{TRSP}} \;\approx\; 1.5 \times 10^{6} \;\;\text{state transitions.} $$

Sizing decisions therefore key off the TRSP arrival rate. The SCP burst matters only as an instantaneous concurrency lower bound (see §6.1).

4. HDFS dependencies and placement assumptions

The reference deployment colocates RegionServers and DataNodes one-to-one across three availability zones. WAL blocks are placed in each of the cluster's three AZs (registered as racks). The loss of one AZ therefore reduces every active write pipeline cluster-wide simultaneously.

The WAL provider should be AsyncWAL, selected by hbase.wal.provider = asyncfs. AsyncFSWAL's single-block-per-file design sidesteps several issues with the legacy FSHLog provider when an AZ is lost. The rest of this document assumes AsyncWAL as the provider and the recommended tuning is applied throughout (full table in §9.6 and §9.7).

5. Mathematical Framework

Every per-stage decision in this document is one of three uses of Little's Law:

$$ L \;=\; \lambda\, W $$

where

  • $L$ = number of items concurrently in the stage (workers, handlers, threads in use),
  • $\lambda$ = arrival rate at the stage,
  • $W$ = time each item spends in the stage (service or hold time).

$L = \lambda W$ is solved for whichever variable is unknown: for thread-pool sizing $L$ is the unknown (metahandler.count, openregion.threads, procedure.threads); for the procedure-store WAL it is $\lambda$ (sustained throughput).

Recovery-window arguments use a separate identity, the fluid-flow drain time

$$ T_{\text{drain}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}}{\mu}, $$

where $W_{\text{total}}$ is the aggregate workload through the stage during recovery and $\mu$ is the stage's service rate. Throughout this document, Little's Law is used to size concurrency (the in-flight population $L$ at each stage) and the fluid-flow equation is used to bound recovery time (how long it takes a stage to drain a known workload). The two are kept notationally distinct: $L$ always denotes a concurrent population, $W$ a per-item time, and $W_{\text{total}}$ a cumulative workload.

A handful of stages are not flow-balance problems but instantaneous burst-concurrency problems. For those the rule is simply $L \geq$ (simultaneous events in the burst) + margin.

6. Per-Stage Derivations

6.1 hbase.master.executor.serverops.threads — burst concurrency

The serverops executor handles inbound server-lifecycle events. At AZ loss, ≈167 dead-server notifications fire near-simultaneously and each must dispatch into an SCP without queuing. This is not a flow problem; it is a one-shot burst:

$$ L_{\text{serverops}} \;\geq\; N_{\text{dead}} \;\approx\; 167 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 256 \;\text{(next power of two, with margin).} $$

At the default of 5, the burst is processed effectively serially.

6.2 hbase.procedure.remote.dispatcher.threadpool.size — burst concurrency

The remote-dispatcher pool runs the per-RS dispatch loops that flush batched RPCs. Concurrency must cover all destinations simultaneously:

$$ L_{\text{disp}} \;\geq\; N_{\text{alive}} \;\approx\; 333 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 512 \;\text{(headroom for retries on transient failures).} $$

Below ~333 the dispatcher itself becomes a bottleneck independent of any other tuning.

6.3 hbase.master.executor.openregion.threads — Little's Law against META

This executor processes reportRegionStateTransition OPEN events. The downstream constraint is the META write rate. With a sustainable META write rate $\lambda_{\text{META}} \approx 5{,}000\,\text{w/s}$ and per-handler hold time $W_{\text{handler}} \approx 50\,\text{ms}$:

$$ L_{\text{open}} \;=\; \lambda_{\text{META}} \cdot W_{\text{handler}} \;=\; 5{,}000 \cdot 0.050 \;\approx\; 250. $$

Anything less and META is left under-utilized. The recommended 256 sits one power of two above this.

6.4 hbase.regionserver.metahandler.count on the master — Little's Law on RPCs

All inbound traffic from RegionServers during recovery is tagged HIGH_QOS by MasterAnnotationReadingPriorityFunction and lands in the master's priority handler pool. Handlers hold for a procedure-store hflush plus region-lock acquisition (≈25 ms typical). Arrival rate during the steepest portion of the recovery curve is ≈5–10K events/sec:

$$ L_{\text{rpc}} \;=\; \lambda_{\text{rpc}} \cdot W_{\text{hold}} \;\approx\; (5\text{–}10) \times 10^{3}\,\text{s}^{-1} \times 0.025\,\text{s} \;\approx\; 125\text{–}250. $$

The recommended 256 covers the upper end with margin.

6.5 hbase.procedure.remote.dispatcher.delay.msec — dwell tax

The dispatcher's dwell timer $d$ is the time it waits to accumulate per-RS RPCs into a single batched flush. During recovery, each remote callable's completion is a serialization point for the next procedure transition, so the dwell tax is paid per transition, per region:

$$ \Delta_{\text{dwell, worst-case}} \;=\; d \cdot t_{\text{TRSP}}. $$

Concretely:

$d$Worst-case dwell per region
150 ms (default)≈ 0.9 s
50 ms (recommended)≈ 0.3 s

Above ~100 ms the cumulative dispatcher dwell becomes a measurable contribution to recovery latency on its own.

6.6 hbase.procedure.store.wal.sync.wait.msec & hbase.master.procedure.threads — closed-loop throughput

The procedure store is a single-writer log (a MasterRegion with an AsyncFSWAL). Each procedure thread blocks once per persist. With $N$ procedure threads, $w$ = sync-wait, and $h$ = hflush latency under stress (≈10 ms), the closed-loop, work-conserving throughput is:

$$ \mu_{\text{proc}} \;\approx\; \frac{N}{w + h} \quad\text{(writes/second).} $$

The drain time for the recovery work follows directly:

$$ T_{\text{drain}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}}{\mu_{\text{proc}}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}\,(w + h)}{N}. $$

At the HBase defaults ($N = 16$, $w = 100\,\text{ms}$, $h = 10\,\text{ms}$), $\mu_{\text{proc}} \approx 145\,\text{w/s}$ — orders of magnitude below the offered rate of the next subsection. The deterministic formula is enough to confirm that $N$ and $w$ must change by orders of magnitude; the actual operating point is set in §7, where service-time variance enters the picture.

Surviving DataNode read load rises $\approx 50\%$ during the recovery window. The DN-side queue inflation pushes the procedure store hflush latency from $\bar{h} \approx 10\,\text{ms}$ to $\bar{h}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 15\,\text{ms}$, derating closed-loop throughput to $\mu_{\text{proc}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 20.5\,\text{K w/s}$ and the corresponding drain time to $T_{\text{proc-drain}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 73\,\text{s}$ (full derivation in §9.8, summary in §4.2).

The procedure store remains the bottleneck stage.

6.7 Offered vs. sustained load

The instantaneous offered rate during the SCP fan-out is:

$$ \lambda_{\text{offered}} \;\approx\; \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}}{T_{\text{create}}} \;=\; \frac{2.5 \times 10^{5}}{5\text{–}10\,\text{s}} \;=\; 25\text{–}50\,\text{K/s.} $$

At the upper end, this exceeds the recommended sustained throughput $\mu_{\text{proc}} \approx 25.6\,\text{K/s}$ (§7.2). The offered burst exceeds the sink for ~5–10 s and the resulting backlog drains over the remainder of the recovery window. The design constraint is that the sink drains $W_{\text{total}}$ within the chosen recovery window.

6.8 hbase.regionserver.executor.openregion.threads — the per-RS bottleneck

Each surviving RegionServer maintains its own pool to execute RS_OPEN_REGION events (and a parallel pool for RS_CLOSE_REGION). The default is 3. During AZ-loss recovery, each surviving RS receives ≈750 OPEN dispatches, and the work each executor thread does for one open is dominated by HDFS reads, not CPU.

Per-RS open throughput:

$$ \mu_{\text{open}}^{\text{(per RS)}} \;=\; \frac{T_{\text{open}}}{W_{\text{open}}} \quad\text{(regions/sec/RS),} $$

where $T_{\text{open}}$ is the configured thread count and $W_{\text{open}}$ is the per-region open time. Cluster-wide:

$$ \mu_{\text{open}}^{\text{(cluster)}} \;=\; N_{\text{alive}} \cdot \mu_{\text{open}}^{\text{(per RS)}} \;=\; \frac{N_{\text{alive}} \cdot T_{\text{open}}}{W_{\text{open}}}. $$

Numerically, with $N_{\text{alive}} = 333$:

With the master-side recommendations of §§6.1–6.7 applied but $T_{\text{open}}$ left at its default of 3, the per-RS open executor is the cluster-wide bottleneck during AZ-loss recovery. The master can persist at 25K/s and dispatch at line-rate, but it pushes into 333 per-RS queues that drain at ≈3 regions/s each at $W_{\text{open}} \approx 1\,\text{s}$ (≈1,000/s cluster-wide; see table above). Effective recovery time is on the order of ≈4 minutes at default $T_{\text{open}}$, and proportionally worse as $W_{\text{open}}$ grows under HDFS stress.

Raising $T_{\text{open}}$ to 32 matches the master's throughput at $W_{\text{open}} \approx 1\,\text{s}$ (the realistic-stress case) and over-provisions the healthy case. The close-region executor should follow symmetrically:

hbase.regionserver.executor.openregion.threads: 3 → 32
hbase.regionserver.executor.closeregion.threads: 3 → 32

The priority-region and meta-region executors operate on a much smaller working set (META replicas plus a handful of system regions) and need only modest bumps:

hbase.regionserver.executor.openpriorityregion.threads: 3 → 8

Inbound RPC handling on the RegionServer also needs headroom during recovery, since the master is sending OPEN/CLOSE RPCs at the new higher rate:

hbase.regionserver.handler.count: 30 → 60

Note that $T_{\text{open}} = 32$ is matched to the NameNode saturation point at the reference $\omega$ and $C_{\text{NN}}$ (§6.9, §8.3): at higher thread counts $W_{\text{open}}$ self-adjusts upward to keep the NN at capacity, with no throughput gain.

The same $\approx 50\%$ DataNode read-load delta of §4 inflates per-region open time to $W_{\text{open}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 1.5 \cdot W_{\text{open}}$, and the close-region path symmetrically. At $T_{\text{open}} = 32$ this puts the realistic-stress $W_{\text{open}}$ at $\approx 1.5\,\text{s}$, dropping cluster-wide open throughput to $\approx 7{,}100$/s and extending the 250K-open drain time to $\approx 35\,\text{s}$. Under the recommended tuning this still sits below the AZ-loss-window procedure-store drain ($T_{\text{proc-drain}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 73\,\text{s}$. Full derivation in §9.8; multiplier summary in §4.2.

The 1.5× inflation above is the mean latency figure. Per region open time has a heavy tail driven by HFile metadata reads from DataNodes. Without read path tuning, an open that picks a not yet classified as dead replica can stall for a full dfs.client.socket-timeout (60 seconds default) before falling back, pushing $W_{\text{open}}$ into multi-second territory and shifting $T_{\text{RS-open}}$ past $T_{\text{proc-drain}}^{\text{AZ}}$ as the cluster wide bottleneck. Hedged reads + JVM-shared DeadNodeDetector (§9.10) bound the tail at $\theta + p_{50,\text{alt}} \approx 150\,\text{ms}$, keeping $W_{\text{open}}$ close to its mean and the stage subordinate to the procedure store. See §9.12 for the per-RS amortized bound.

6.9 HDFS NameNode & DataNode — the cluster-wide concurrency ceiling

RegionServer-side parallelism is bounded above by HDFS. A region open is HDFS-heavy: roughly $\omega \approx 30$ NameNode operations (one getFileInfo + one getBlockLocations per HFile, multiplied across HFiles per column family per region) plus per-block reads from DataNodes for footers, indexes, and bloom filters. Under AZ loss it also includes WAL-split recovered edits.

Treat the NameNode as a single shared resource with op-throughput ceiling $C_{\text{NN}}$. At $C_{\text{NN}} \approx 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$ (see §8.3) and $\omega = 30$, the cluster sustains $\approx 10{,}000$ concurrent opens at $W_{\text{open}} = 1\,\text{s}$, about 30 per surviving RS, so $T_{\text{open}} = 32$ is matched to the NameNode saturation point. Pushing $T_{\text{open}}$ higher does not increase throughput. $W_{\text{open}}$ self-adjusts upward to keep the NN at capacity, and the system finds its own fixed point at that operating limit.

The HDFS contribution to recovery time at the NN saturation limit is

$$ T_{\text{NN}} \;=\; \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}} \cdot \omega}{C_{\text{NN}}} \;=\; \frac{2.5 \times 10^{5} \cdot 30}{3 \times 10^{5}} \;\approx\; 25\,\text{s.} $$

At the recommended HBase tuning the master procedure store contributes $T_{\text{proc}} \approx 59\,\text{s}$ (§7.2), so HDFS sits below the system-wide bottleneck at the reference workload. The recommended deployment (§8.3) is sized to keep $T_{\text{NN}} < T_{\text{proc}}$ at the reference $\omega$.

$T_{\text{NN}}$ as derived above is the throughput bound contribution at NN saturation. It does not include the per call wait that the embedded DFSClient spends on a hung previously active NN during HDFS HA failover, which is governed at the client side by ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms (default $120\,\text{s}$, tuned $30\,\text{s}$ in §9.11) rather than by dfs.client.failover.*. The HDFS side failover wall clock time is unchanged by anything in hbase-site.xml. See the §8.4 "with NN failover" bracket. §9.11 presents the layered budget that combines both terms.

6.10 hbase.regionserver.wal.max.splitters — distributed WAL splitting

Each dead RS leaves an unarchived WAL set that the master must split before its regions can be reassigned. Per dead RS, the master enumerates the files in WALs/<server>-splitting/, creates one SplitWALProcedure per WAL, and dispatches each as a child SplitWALRemoteProcedure to a worker chosen by the WorkerAssigner. The worker runs the split on its RS_LOG_REPLAY_OPS executor: a sequential HDFS read of the WAL plus 3-replicated writes of the per-region recovered.edits files. The master polls for completion (the source WAL no longer existing at its splitting-dir path) before dispatching the corresponding TRSPs.

The same configuration key, hbase.regionserver.wal.max.splitters (default 2), gates two things: the per-RS slot count tracked by the WorkerAssigner on the master, and the core pool size of the RS_LOG_REPLAY_OPS executor on each RS. Cluster-wide split parallelism is therefore:

$$ P_{\text{split}} \;=\; N_{\text{alive}} \cdot K, \qquad K \,=\, \texttt{hbase.regionserver.wal.max.splitters}. $$

These quantities define the WAL workload:

  • $N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ — unarchived WAL files per dead RS at outage time. Workload-dependent, capped above by hbase.regionserver.maxlogs (default $\geq 32$).
  • $M_{\text{wal}} = N_{\text{dead}} \cdot N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ — total WAL files to split. At the reference workload, $M_{\text{wal}}$ ranges from $\approx 1{,}300$ (typical write load, $N_{\text{wal/RS}} \approx 8$) to $\approx 5{,}300$ (write-heavy, $N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ approaching maxlogs).
  • $W_{\text{split}}$ — per-WAL split service time, HDFS-bound by a sequential read of $S$ bytes plus 3-replicated recovered.edits writes of up to $S$ bytes, with $S$ bounded above by logrollsize (default $\approx 128\,\text{MiB}$). Empirically $\approx 3\,\text{s}$ with healthy DataNodes, rising to $\approx 15\,\text{s}$ under DataNode contention.

Applying Little's Law to the split-slot pool — $M_{\text{wal}}$ items of mean service $W_{\text{split}}$ through $P_{\text{split}}$ servers — the WAL-splitting drain time is:

$$ T_{\text{WAL}} \;=\; \frac{M_{\text{wal}} \cdot W_{\text{split}}}{P_{\text{split}}} \;=\; \frac{N_{\text{dead}} \cdot N_{\text{wal/RS}} \cdot W_{\text{split}}}{N_{\text{alive}} \cdot K}. $$

Numerical points at $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$, $N_{\text{alive}} = 333$:

$K$$N_{\text{wal/RS}}$$W_{\text{split}}$$T_{\text{WAL}}$

At typical write loads ($N_{\text{wal/RS}} \approx 8$) WAL splitting at default $K = 2$ contributes ≈ 10 s — comparable to the SCP-burst pipeline allowance but still well below $T_{\text{proc}}$. Under sustained write load ($N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ approaching maxlogs) the default contributes ${\sim}80\,$s and rivals the master procedure store as the dominant term. Raising $K$ from 2 to 8 quadruples per-RS split parallelism while keeping per-RS HDFS bandwidth in budget: each split slot does one sequential read plus a 3-replicated write at sustained ≈ 50–100 MB/s, so eight concurrent slots remain comfortably inside the 25 Gbps NIC envelope of the reference instance.

$W_{\text{split}}$ is an exogenous, HDFS-bound cost that HBase cannot directly tune. It is reduced indirectly by smaller WAL roll size (more files of smaller $W_{\text{split}}$ each, with $T_{\text{WAL}}$ unchanged in aggregate) or by holding $N_{\text{wal/RS}}$ down via more aggressive flush policy.

WAL splitting and region opens overlap at the cluster level. SCPs run independently, so once an early dead RS finishes splitting its WALs its regions can be assigned and opened while later SCPs are still splitting. Both stages draw on the same surviving DataNodes for HDFS bandwidth, so a higher $T_{\text{WAL}}$ inflates the effective $W_{\text{open}}$ during the WAL-split window. The model treats $T_{\text{WAL}}$ as a parallel stage in the recovery max (§8.4) rather than additive to $T_{\text{RS-open}}$.

Surviving DataNodes carry $\approx 50\%$ more read traffic, which inflates split-WAL read latency and the parallel HFile-open reads in the same proportion. The aggregate WAL-split drain time scales to $T_{\text{WAL}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 1.5 \cdot T_{\text{WAL}}$, and the resulting effective $W_{\text{open}}$ during the WAL-split window picks up the same factor on top of any baseline inflation (§6.8's callout). Full derivation in §9.8. Multiplier summary in §4.2.

The 1.5× multiplier captures the queueing inflation on the surviving DataNodes’ sequential read throughput. The tail is what produces the $W_{\text{split}} \approx 15\,\text{s}$ "DataNode contention" entry in the third bullet of this subsection, a sequential WAL read that picks a not-yet-classified-dead replica blocks for a full dfs.client.socket-timeout before falling back, even when the average DN throughput would have completed the read in $\approx 5\,\text{s}$. Read-path tuning (§9.10) does not change the +50% mean-throughput cost, because the surviving DNs still carry the same aggregate read traffic, but it does collapse the tail back toward the healthy $W_{\text{split}} \approx 3\text{--}5\,\text{s}$ regime by capping individual slow reads at $\theta + p_{50,\text{alt}} \approx 150\,\text{ms}$ via hedged reads. The cross section impact is in §9.12.

7. Bulk-service queueing analysis of the procedure store

The closed-loop throughput formula in §6.6 treats service time as deterministic, which is sufficient to size $\mu$ but not to size headroom. The procedure-store hflush is not deterministic: $p_{50} \approx 7$ ms, $p_{99} \gtrsim 25$ ms under stress. Since each flush cycle serves up to $N$ writes in bulk, the right model is an $M/G^{[N]}/1$ bulk-service queue, and the design rule must guard against per-cycle arrival overspill rather than per-write wait.

The cycle-level service-time setup is:

  • Service time per cycle: $S = w + h$, where $w$ is the dwell wait (deterministic) and $h$ is the hflush latency (random).
  • $E[S] = w + \bar{h}$, $\;\text{Var}(S) = \sigma_h^{2}$, $\;C_S^{2} = \sigma_h^{2}/(w+\bar{h})^{2}$.
  • Each cycle serves up to $N$ procedure writes. Cluster throughput: $\mu = N / E[S]$. Per-write utilization: $\rho = \lambda E[S] / N$.

For $w = 10\,\text{ms}$, $\bar{h} = 10\,\text{ms}$, $\sigma_h \approx 8\,\text{ms}$ (consistent with $p_{99} \approx 30\,\text{ms}$):

$$ E[S] = 20\,\text{ms},\quad C_S^{2} = \frac{64}{400} = 0.16. $$

7.1 Design rule: bulk-cycle variance bound

In a bulk queue, macroscopic queueing delay (multi-cycle backlog) only occurs if the number of arrivals during a single flush cycle exceeds $N$, causing writes to spill over into the next cycle. The relevant tail to bound is therefore the per-cycle arrival overflow probability, not the per-write residual wait.

To bound sustainable utilization, size the headroom such that capacity $N$ covers the expected arrivals per cycle plus a safety margin to absorb variance in hflush latency. For a cycle of random length $S = w + h$, the number of Poisson arrivals $A$ during the cycle satisfies:

  • Mean: $\mu_A = \lambda\,E[S] = \rho N$.
  • Variance: $\sigma_A^{2} = \lambda\,E[S] + \lambda^{2}\sigma_h^{2} = \rho N + \rho^{2} N^{2} C_S^{2}$ (Poisson term plus the law of total variance contribution from random $S$).

Adopt the design rule that capacity $N$ must cover the mean plus a $1.33\sigma$ safety margin (so $\approx 90\%$ of cycles clear completely without spillover, even during slow flushes):

$$ N \;\geq\; \mu_A + 1.33\,\sigma_A. $$

Substituting $N = 512$ and $C_S^{2} = 0.16$, evaluate at a target utilization of $\rho = 0.65$:

$$ \begin{aligned} \mu_A &= 0.65 \cdot 512 \;\approx\; 333, \\ \sigma_A^{2} &= 333 + (0.65)^{2}(512)^{2}(0.16) \;\approx\; 18{,}054 \;\Longrightarrow\; \sigma_A \;\approx\; 134, \\ \mu_A + 1.33\,\sigma_A &= 333 + 1.33 \cdot 134 \;\approx\; 511.2 \;\leq\; 512. \end{aligned} $$

This pins the maximum sustainable steady-state utilization to $\rho_{\max} \approx 0.65$. Operating above this threshold sharply increases the probability of multi-cycle overspill: as $\rho \to 1$ the arrival mean approaches $N$ while $\sigma_A$ continues to grow, so an increasing fraction of cycles spill into the next, and macroscopic backlog accumulates. The same $\rho_{\max} \approx 0.65$ engineering target is also the right margin to absorb physical jitter (JVM GC pauses, transient DataNode pipeline stalls) that inflate $\sigma_h$ above its nominal value.

Under the strict one-replica-per-AZ placement of §4, the inflated DataNode read load pushes $\bar{h}$ to $\bar{h}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 15\,\text{ms}$ and $\sigma_h$ up roughly in proportion, so $C_S^{2}$ rises from 0.16 to $C_S^{2,\text{AZ}} \approx 0.23$. Re-evaluating the variance bound at $N = 512$ and $C_S^{2,\text{AZ}} = 0.23$ shrinks the maximum sustainable utilization to $\rho_{\max}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 0.61$, about $0.94\times$ the steady-state value. Full derivation in §9.8; multiplier summary in §4.2.

7.2 Recommended sizing

Under static thread-pool sizing, $N$ is chosen to bound the procedure-store drain time at saturation:

$$ T_{\text{proc-drain}} \;=\; \frac{W_{\text{total}}\,(w+h)}{N}. $$

Targeting $T_{\text{proc-drain}} \approx 60\,\text{s}$ at $w = h = 10\,\text{ms}$ gives $N \approx 500$. Rounded to the nearest power of two, the recommended operating point is

$$ N \;=\; 512,\quad w \;=\; 10\,\text{ms},\quad \bar{h} \;=\; 10\,\text{ms,} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mu_{\text{proc}} \;\approx\; 25.6\,\text{K w/s,}\;\; T_{\text{proc-drain}} \;\approx\; 59\,\text{s.} $$

The bulk-cycle variance bound (§7.1) caps the sustainable post-burst arrival rate at $\lambda \leq \rho_{\max}\mu = 0.65 \cdot 25.6\,\text{K} \approx 16.6\,\text{K w/s}$. During the SCP fan-out transient (§6.7) the procedure store runs at $\rho \approx 1$ — burst arrivals briefly exceed $\mu$ for 5–10 s and the resulting queue empties over the next ~60 s. The variance bound applies to the post-burst steady state. The drain-time bound applies during the transient.

This is the upper bound for static sizing. The longer-term direction is core/overflow dynamic sizing (§12) with the static value here as the cap.

8.3 HDFS NameNode capacity

HDFS NameNode op-throughput sets the cluster-wide concurrency ceiling for region opens (§6.9). $C_{\text{NN}}$ depends on instance sizing, JVM and HDFS configuration, and namespace topology, and is tunable from outside HBase before it becomes the bottleneck.

Instance

Run the active NameNode on m7g.16xlarge (64 vCPU AWS Graviton3, 256 GB DDR5-4800, 30 Gbps network, 20 Gbps EBS bandwidth). Three properties of this instance matter for region-open bursts:

  • 64 vCPUs support a 128-thread IPC handler pool (dfs.namenode.handler.count = 128) and a wide reader pool, so RPC processing parallelism scales with the concurrent open-RPC fan-in.
  • 256 GB of DDR5 hosts a ~200 GB JVM heap, enough to keep the full namespace in memory at reference scale, with headroom for off-heap structures and OS page cache.
  • DDR5-4800 memory bandwidth is what bounds the in-memory inode-tree traversal that dominates getFileInfo and getBlockLocations, the two operations that together account for the $\omega \approx 30$ region-open footprint.

HDFS tuning

  • dfs.namenode.fslock.fair = false — allows reads to barge past contending writers, which is appropriate when region-open traffic dominates.
  • dfs.namenode.handler.count = 128 and ipc.server.read.threadpool.size = 16, so handler-pool width is not the bottleneck below the lock.
  • JournalNodes' local edits directory on NVMe (instance store or io2), isolated from HBase DataNode block storage, so JN edit-log fsyncs do not contend with the DataNode block write/close traffic from the HBase WAL pipeline.

Sustained throughput

With the instance and tuning above, a single active NameNode sustains

$$ C_{\text{NN}} \;\approx\; 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s} \quad\text{(read-heavy, single active NameNode, tuned).} $$

This is the value used throughout the model.

Load during the partial-failure window

At the moment of AZ loss the NameNode sees a brief synchronous burst of addBlock RPCs as every surviving RegionServer rolls its WAL onto a fresh width-2 pipeline. The roll cascade completes within $T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \approx 6\,\text{s}$ (the AsyncFSWAL slow-packet ladder derived in §9.8), so the cluster-wide rate peaks at $\lambda_{\text{addBlock}}^{\text{burst}} \approx 55$/s. Against the tuned ceiling $C_{\text{NN}} \approx 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$ this is under 0.02% utilization and disappears below the noise floor; it is a transient blip rather than a sustained load shift.

On a longer timescale the NameNode absorbs the consequences of $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ DataNodes ceasing to heartbeat. Each lost DN is marked dead only after dfs.namenode.heartbeat.recheck-interval $\approx 5\,\text{min}$, at which point the per-DN dead transition fires the block-management callbacks that scan its blocks and queue them for re-replication. Averaged over the recheck window this is $\approx N_{\text{dead}} / 300\,\text{s} \approx 0.6$ dead-DN events per second. The actual arrivals are bunched at the back end of the window as the staggered heartbeat timeouts come due, but even the local burst is small relative to $C_{\text{NN}}$ and well within the NameNode's handler-pool width.

The dead-DN scans produce a re-replication backlog corresponding to the lost AZ's share of the namespace. The NameNode schedules transfer commands at no more than dfs.namenode.replication.max-streams-hard-limit per surviving DataNode, so the NameNode-side scheduler load is bounded by that throttle regardless of backlog size and likewise stays well clear of $C_{\text{NN}}$.

The aggregate of these additions is comfortably below $C_{\text{NN}}$ at the reference workload.

Recovery-time contribution

At the reference $\omega = 30$ and $C_{\text{NN}} = 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$:

$$ T_{\text{NN}} \;=\; \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}\,\omega}{C_{\text{NN}}} \;=\; \frac{2.5 \times 10^{5} \cdot 30}{3 \times 10^{5}} \;\approx\; 25\,\text{s.} $$

$T_{\text{NN}}$ sits below the master procedure store drain time $T_{\text{proc}} \approx 59\,\text{s}$ (§7.2), so HDFS is not the system-wide bottleneck at the reference workload.

8.4 Bounded recovery-time estimates

Recovery time is the max of per-stage drain times plus a small pipeline overhead, so the model gives a closed-form bound at the reference workload ($N=500$, $R=1{,}500$, $f=\tfrac{1}{3}$, $M_{\text{TRSP}}=250{,}000$, $W_{\text{total}}=1.5\times 10^{6}$). At the recommended settings of §7.1–§7.2, four stage rates set the operating bound:

$$ \begin{aligned} T_{\text{proc}} &= \frac{W_{\text{total}}(w+h)}{N_{\text{proc}}} = \frac{1.5\times 10^{6}\cdot 0.020}{512} \;\approx\; 59\,\text{s,} \\[2pt] T_{\text{WAL}} &= \frac{N_{\text{dead}}\, N_{\text{wal/RS}}\, W_{\text{split}}}{N_{\text{alive}}\, K} = \frac{167\, N_{\text{wal/RS}}\, W_{\text{split}}}{333 \cdot 8} \;\approx\; 0.063\,N_{\text{wal/RS}}\, W_{\text{split}}\,\text{s,} \\[2pt] T_{\text{RS-open}} &= \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}\,W_{\text{open}}}{N_{\text{alive}}\,T_{\text{open}}} = \frac{250{,}000\,W_{\text{open}}}{333\cdot 32} \;\approx\; 23.5\,W_{\text{open}}\,\text{s,} \\[2pt] T_{\text{NN}} &= \frac{M_{\text{TRSP}}\,\omega}{C_{\text{NN}}}. \end{aligned} $$

$T_{\text{proc}}$ is fixed by tuning. $T_{\text{WAL}}$ depends on the workload-driven WAL count per dead RS and the HDFS-bound per-WAL service time. $T_{\text{RS-open}}$ and $T_{\text{NN}}$ are both functions of the HDFS operating point ($\omega$, $C_{\text{NN}}$, $W_{\text{open}}$), and $W_{\text{open}}$ rises until either the NN saturates or the per-RS thread pool fills, so in practice the larger of $T_{\text{RS-open}}$ and $T_{\text{NN}}$ binds. WAL splitting and region opens overlap at the cluster level (see §6.10), so $T_{\text{WAL}}$ enters the recovery max as a parallel stage rather than additively. With a small allowance $T_{\text{pipe}} \approx 10\,\text{s}$ for the SCP fan-out burst:

$$ T_{\text{recovery}} \;\approx\; \max\!\big(T_{\text{proc}},\,T_{\text{WAL}},\,T_{\text{RS-open}},\,T_{\text{NN}}\big) \;+\; T_{\text{pipe}}. $$

With NameNode failover

The scenarios above assume the active HDFS NameNode survives the AZ outage. If the active was in the failed AZ, HA failover to the standby in a surviving AZ sits on the critical path before HBase recovery can make meaningful progress, because region opens depend on getFileInfo/getBlockLocations from a healthy NameNode. Community operational experience with production HDFS HA at comparable cluster size places the failover wall-clock in the bracket

$$ T_{\text{NN-failover}} \;\approx\; 90\text{–}180\,\text{s,} $$

dominated by ZKFC fencing and ZK session expiry (~30 s at a standard 30-second ZK session timeout), standby promotion and edit-log catch-up (~10–20 s for a current standby), and DataNode block-report quorum to the new active before safe-mode exit (~60–120 s; scaling with total block count, the dominant variable at this cluster size). When the NameNode is also lost, the operational recovery wall-clock for the typical case is therefore

$$ T_{\text{recovery}}^{\text{(typical, with NN failover)}} \;\approx\; T_{\text{NN-failover}} + T_{\text{recovery}}^{\text{(typical)}} \;\approx\; \mathbf{2.5\text{–}4.5\,\text{min.}} $$

The lower end of this bracket corresponds to a fast HA failover (current standby, prompt block reports) followed by a procedure-store-bound HBase recovery in ~70 s. The upper end is a slow block-report quorum (~2 min) followed by a procedure-store-bound recovery in ~90 s.

$T_{\text{NN-failover}}$ above is the HDFS side wall clock time only. At HDFS defaults this adds up to $120\,\text{s}$ per stuck call to the bracket. With the recommended §9.11 tuning ($30\,\text{s}$ RPC timeout, $5\,\text{s}$ connect timeout) the residual collapses to ${\le}\,30\,\text{s}$.

9. HDFS pipeline tuning for degraded dependencies

This section addresses a related failure mode where a number of DataNodes are degraded rather than completely offline, perhaps due to EBS level degradation during an AZ outage or partial outage. Embedded HDFS clients inside the RegionServers keep selecting these DataNodes for new WAL pipelines, resulting in a sustained tax on WAL hflush latency that propagates to client visible tail latency for every region whose WAL passes through a sick pipeline.

FSHLog's DFSOutputStream reacts to a pipeline drop by invoking ReplaceDatanodeOnFailure, which adds complexity and latency to the write path during AZ loss. AsyncFSWAL takes a different tack and instead each WAL file is exactly one block long, so on any DataNode error the upper layer immediately rolls the WAL and opens a fresh file with a fresh addBlock. The NameNode satisfies the new pipeline at width 2 (subject to dfs.namenode.replication.min), and the writer continues. The document therefore recommends AsyncWAL and the quantitative cross-impacts in §9.8 assume it.

The section is organized as a one-paragraph model of the embedded client write pipeline (§9.1), the detection window arithmetic that explains why a degraded DN keeps being picked (§9.2), the divergent pipeline recovery behavior of the two HBase WAL providers (§9.3, §9.4), the HBase-side circuit breakers (§9.5), tuning recommendations at the HDFS layer (§9.6) and HBase layer (§9.7), the cross-section impact under strict one-replica-per-AZ placement (§9.8) and its limitations (§9.9), and the symmetric read-path tuning (§9.10), NameNode-RPC tuning (§9.11), and read-path cross-section impact (§9.12) that bound the slow-tail residual the §9.8 mean-latency analysis cannot capture.

9.1 The embedded HDFS client write pipeline

Every WAL append crosses an embedded HDFS write pipeline of replication DataNodes (3 in the reference deployment). Pipeline construction is performed by the NameNode's BlockPlacementPolicy at addBlock time, honoring the client-supplied excludedNodes set. Pipeline recovery is governed by a client-side state machine that differs sharply between the two HBase WAL providers. The provider is selected by hbase.wal.provider (default defaultProvider ≈ asyncfs, alternative filesystem ≈ FSHLog).

NameNode ↑ addBlock(src, excludedNodes) | returns LocatedBlock[DN1, DN2, DN3] | RS WAL writer | | | v v DFSClient ------------------------------------------------------+ | data | DN1 head -------------> DN2 mirror -------------> DN3 tail ^ ^ | | | | +-------- ack ------------+----------- ack --------------+ FSHLog/DFSClient chained-pipeline topology shown. AsyncFSWAL's FanOutOneBlockAsyncDFSOutput replaces the head->mirror->tail chain with parallel client->DN channels; the NameNode-side addBlock half is identical.

On FSHLog the writer is a stock DFSOutputStream. Pipeline failures trigger HDFS's pipeline recovery machinery. On AsyncFSWAL the writer is HBase's own FanOutOneBlockAsyncDFSOutput, which avoids the complexity and latency of pipeline recovery by only ever writing files the length of a single block. This allows it to open a Netty channel per replica, fan packets out in parallel, and immediately recover at the HBase level with a WAL roll.

9.2 Partial-failure detection windows

Various NameNode-side mechanisms can keep a degraded DN out of new pipelines. At HDFS defaults each has a different detection window and a different set of opt-ins.

Dead-DN window

The NameNode declares a DN dead only after a long no-heartbeat window:

$$ T_{\text{dead}} \;=\; 2\,t_{\text{recheck}} \;+\; 10\,t_{\text{hb}}. $$

With defaults dfs.namenode.heartbeat.recheck-interval = 5 min and dfs.heartbeat.interval = 3 s, this is 10 min 30 s (computed in DatanodeManager at construction, checked in isDatanodeDead). Until the dead window expires, the DN remains IN_SERVICE and the placement policy will keep proposing it.

Stale-DN window

dfs.namenode.stale.datanode.interval (default 30 s) marks a DN stale after a missed heartbeat or two. Stale DNs are avoided as targets for new pipelines if dfs.namenode.avoid.write.stale.datanode is true. In this production deployment that switch is set, so the 30 s window is active and the placement policy excludes a DN as soon as it misses heartbeats long enough to go stale. A safety hatch (dfs.namenode.write.stale.datanode.ratio, default 0.5) disables stale-DN avoidance when more than half the cluster is stale, so during a broad partition the protection disengages exactly when it matters most.

The stale-DN avoidance only fires when a DN actually misses heartbeats. A degraded DN continues to heartbeat normally and never goes stale. The slow-peer graylist below is the only NN-side mechanism that catches this case.

Slow-peer graylist

Each DN computes an outlier set over its downstream send latencies (DataNodePeerMetrics with OutlierDetector) and ships them in heartbeats. The NameNode aggregates votes in SlowPeerTracker, a periodic daemon materializes a UUID set, and BlockPlacementPolicyDefault.isGoodDatanode rejects DNs in that set during placement. Three opt-ins are required, all default false: dfs.datanode.peer.stats.enabled (DN-side reporting), dfs.namenode.block-placement-policy.exclude-slow-nodes.enabled (NN-side enforcement), and a non-zero dfs.namenode.slowpeer.collect.interval on the materialization daemon. Worst-case time from when the DN begins to misbehave to when the DN appears in the graylist:

$$ T_{\text{graylist}} \;\lesssim\; 3\,t_{\text{outliers.report.interval}} \;+\; t_{\text{slowpeer.collect.interval}}. $$

At defaults (30m, 30m) this is ≈ 2 hours. Even with peer stats enabled, the exclude-slow-nodes switch controls whether the NN actually refuses the DN. Out of the box the NN sees the slow-peer reports but does nothing with them.

Load-based exclusion

dfs.namenode.redundancy.considerLoad (default true, factor 2.0) skips a DN whose xceiverCount exceeds the cluster average by the factor. A DN with a hot disk or facing network degradation slows packet acks without inflating xceiver counts.

9.3 FSHLog: traditional DFSClient pipeline recovery

For deployments still on FSHLog (i.e. those that have not yet switched to the AsyncWAL provider recommended in §4), the writer is a stock DFSOutputStream / DataStreamer and inherits HDFS's pipeline-recovery state machine. This subsection documents that machinery and the FSHLog-side configuration that controls it. The cross-section impact numbers in §9.8 assume AsyncWAL.

The client maintains a per-stream excludedNodes cache, populated whenever a pipeline DN fails and passed into the next addBlock call so the NameNode's placement policy skips the bad DN when allocating the next block. Entries expire after dfs.client.write.exclude.nodes.cache.expiry.interval.millis (default 10 min), giving a freshly-failed DN a window to demonstrate it is back to health before being reconsidered.

The replace-datanode-on-failure policy (governed by the four keys dfs.client.block.write.replace-datanode-on-failure.{enable, policy, min-replication, best-effort}) decides what to do when a DN drops mid-write. The client can ask the NameNode for an additional DN via getAdditionalDatanode and grow the surviving pipeline back to a chosen replication floor, instead of continuing to stream against a shrunken set.

After a DN failure the client drives a generation-stamp recovery by calling updateBlockForPipeline. The NameNode bumps the block's generation stamp and persists the new expected-locations set to the edit log; the client then opens a new pipeline with the bumped GS on the surviving DNs (plus any replacement obtained in step two) and resumes streaming. The block itself is not abandoned, and append throughput is interrupted only for the duration of the RPC round-trips rather than restarted from a roll.

The state machine that ties these together lives in DataStreamer:

STREAMING [normal: streaming packets to all DNs] | | ack with ERROR / socket timeout / closed peer v DATA_STREAMER_ERROR_STATE | | handleBadDatanode() — drop the failed DN from `nodes[]` v |---> (surviving ≥ replace-datanode-min) | | | | setupPipelineForAppendOrRecovery() | | | | | |--- dtpReplaceDatanodeOnFailure.satisfy() = true | | | | | | | | addDatanode2ExistingPipeline() | | | | getAdditionalDatanode(excludedNodes ∪ failed DN) | | | v | | | transfer replica to the new DN | | v | | updateBlockForPipeline() / updatePipeline() — bump GS | v | STREAMING (resumed on new pipeline) | `---> (surviving < replace-datanode-min) and best-effort = false v FAIL — throw IOException upward; HBase WAL.sync() fails

The relevant client-side knobs all live in HdfsClientConfigKeys.BlockWrite.ReplaceDatanodeOnFailure. Defaults: enable = true, policy = DEFAULT, min-replication = 0, best-effort = false. The DEFAULT policy attempts a replacement when the pipeline shrinks below the original replication factor on a non-append write. ALWAYS attempts on every failure. NEVER disables replacement entirely.

FSHLog tolerates a partial pipeline failure provided the NN can find a replacement DN. The cost is one GS bump and one extra addBlock round-trip. The WAL file does not need to roll, and append throughput is briefly interrupted (one packet round-trip plus the GS-bump RPC) rather than restarted.

Otherwise, FSHLog.doCheckLogLowReplication forces a roll when the current block has fewer than hbase.regionserver.hlog.tolerable.lowreplication live replicas. This catches the case where the pipeline silently degrades below that floor. The key defaults to the cluster replication factor (typically 3), which during an AZ-loss event interacts badly with the NameNode's block placement. Roll churn is bounded by hbase.regionserver.hlog.lowreplication.rolllimit (default 5), so the mechanism cannot loop forever. Lowering the floor to 2 (see §9.7) eliminates the WAL roll storm.

9.4 AsyncFSWAL: fan-out pipeline and ExcludeDatanodeManager

AsyncFSWAL replaces the stock client with FanOutOneBlockAsyncDFSOutput, in hbase-asyncfs. Each WAL file is bounded to exactly one HDFS block, sidestepping the entire chained pipeline construction and pipeline recovery machinery of the standard HDFS client. HBase's custom client opens one Netty channel per replica and streams packets to all DNs in parallel. There is no head/mirror/tail relationship between the DNs. The healthy-case benefit is that hflush wait drops by a small constant factor, because there is no serial-hop tail latency, and the recovery case is greatly simplified.

AsyncFSWAL ignores dfs.client.block.write.replace-datanode-on-failure.*. On any DN error, all pending acks fail, and the upper layer immediately rolls the WAL. Because each WAL file is one block, this is as simple as closing the broken block-as-file, opening a fresh file with a fresh addBlock, and starting a fresh fan-out.

For the roll to be productive, hbase-asyncfs carries its own degradation tracker. StreamSlowMonitor observes per-packet ack latency on each in-flight WAL writer and decides when a DN's behavior is degraded enough to act on, and ExcludeDatanodeManager holds the resulting per-RegionServer registry of DNs to avoid.

The slow-packet decision in StreamSlowMonitor.checkProcessTimeAndSpeed uses two thresholds keyed off packet size, on the principle that small packets are bounded by per-RPC latency while large packets are bounded by throughput. Packets below hbase.regionserver.async.wal.datanode.slow.check.speed.packet.data.length.min (default 64 KiB) are judged against hbase.regionserver.async.wal.datanode.slow.packet.process.time.millis (default 6 000 ms). Larger packets are judged against hbase.regionserver.async.wal.datanode.slow.packet.speed.min.kbs (default 20 KB/s). Either threshold being crossed contributes one observation against the DN.

The design assumes any DN can have an occasional bad packet under GC or transient network jitter. Observations are accumulated per DN in a sliding deque whose validity window is hbase.regionserver.async.wal.slow.detect.data.ttl.ms (default 10 min), and only when a DN accumulates hbase.regionserver.async.wal.min.slow.detect.count (default 3) live observations is it promoted to the exclude registry. The result is a quorum of slow packets across a sliding window, not an instantaneous reaction to any single bad packet, a deliberate trade-off of detection latency against false-positive immunity.

ExcludeDatanodeManager holds at most hbase.regionserver.async.wal.max.exclude.datanode.count (default 3) DataNodes, each retained for hbase.regionserver.async.wal.exclude.datanode.info.ttl.hour (default 6 hours) before automatic eviction. On each subsequent WAL roll the current registry contents are passed as the excludedNodes argument to the NameNode's addBlock call, so the placement policy is barred from returning those DNs in the new file's pipeline.

Because ExcludeDatanodeManager is instantiated per RegionServer process, a bad DN is rediscovered independently by each surviving RegionServer, once the slow packet quorum has accumulated against that DN from that RS's own WAL traffic, so cluster wide convergence takes as many independent detections as there are RegionServers writing through the bad DN, with the per RS detection latency set by min.slow.detect.count = 3 divided by that RS's per-DN slow packet rate. The NameNode graylist (§9.2) is the only mechanism with true cluster wide scope.

Also, the registry is merely a bounded cache. With max.exclude.datanode.count = 3, only three DataNodes can be suppressed at any moment, and a wider incident causes thrashing as newly detected bad DNs evict additional bad ones from the cache. The cap is appropriate for the steady state assumption that DN failures are uncorrelated and few at a time, but it is the wrong approach for an AZ-scoped incident. To be resilient against wider scale failures, the cap should be raised (see §9.7) so that the registry can hold the full set of concurrently degraded DNs without losing coverage.

9.8 Cross-section impact under strict one-replica-per-AZ placement

The HDFS-side mechanics in §9.1–§9.7 explain what happens to one degraded pipeline. The cluster-wide impact summarized in §4 and used as inline callouts in §6.6, §6.8, §6.10, §7.1, and §8.3 follows from combining that mechanics with the strict one-replica-per-AZ placement constraint and an upper bound on the immediate WAL roll burst. This subsection derives those numbers end-to-end.

Setup

At the moment one of three AZs is lost, $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ DataNodes vanish, and every active WAL pipeline cluster-wide instantly drops one replica. Because each pipeline started at width $r = 3$ with one replica per AZ, $N_{\text{pipelines-degraded}} = N_{\text{alive}} = 333$ — every surviving RegionServer's open WAL is affected simultaneously. AsyncFSWAL's StreamSlowMonitor sees the IO failure on the lost-AZ replica, the upper layer closes the current width-3 file via §9.5's low-replication and slow-sync paths, opens a fresh width-2 file via addBlock, and resumes appending. Recommended tuning (§9.7) keeps the new pipeline acceptable to both the NameNode (dfs.namenode.replication.min = 2) and the low-replication-roll guard (hbase.regionserver.hlog.tolerable.lowreplication = 2), so the surviving cluster reaches a steady state of width-2 WAL writers within seconds of the AZ loss.

WAL roll burst at AZ loss

Detection-to-roll latency on the lost-AZ replica is bounded by §9.5's AsyncFSWAL slow-packet ladder. Three consecutive packets at the slow-packet threshold trip StreamSlowMonitor, after which the upper layer rolls on the next sync. At the recommended async.wal.datanode.slow.packet.process.time.millis = 2000 and async.wal.min.slow.detect.count = 3:

$$ T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \;\approx\; N_{\text{min}} \cdot T_{\text{slow-packet}} \;=\; 3 \cdot 2\,\text{s} \;=\; 6\,\text{s.} $$

$N_{\text{pipelines-degraded}} = 333$ surviving RegionServers each issue exactly one addBlock RPC over that interval, giving a cluster-wide burst rate:

$$ \lambda_{\text{addBlock}}^{\text{burst}} \;\approx\; \frac{N_{\text{alive}}}{T_{\text{roll-trigger}}} \;=\; \frac{333}{6\,\text{s}} \;\approx\; 55\,\text{addBlock/s.} $$

This is negligible against the tuned NameNode ceiling $C_{\text{NN}} \approx 3 \times 10^{5}\,\text{op/s}$ (§8.3). It does, however, transition the cluster's WAL writers from width 3 to width 2 in a single bounded interval, which sets the stage for the read-load delta and abort budget below.

Bounded RS abort count

A surviving RegionServer aborts when an in-flight WAL sync fails to acknowledge within hbase.regionserver.wal.sync.timeout (see §9.5). Under AsyncWAL the bad-replica error surfaces within $T_{\text{roll-trigger}} \approx 6\,\text{s}$ and the writer rolls onto a healthy width-2 pipeline, well below the recommended wal.sync.timeout of 60 seconds, so the watchdog ordinarily never fires. An abort therefore requires the residual case where the slow-tail conditions persist through the roll and the new pipeline also fails to ack within the watchdog window. Bounding this by the steady state slow-sync tail probability $P_{\text{slow-sync}} \lesssim 0.01$, a generous upper bound on the recommended tuning P99:

$$ P_{\text{at-risk}} \;\lesssim\; P_{\text{slow-sync}} \;\approx\; 0.01, \qquad N_{\text{abort}} \;\lesssim\; N_{\text{alive}} \cdot P_{\text{at-risk}} \;\approx\; 333 \cdot 0.01 \;\lesssim\; 3. $$

That is, the operator's tail-risk budget for the surviving AZs is on the order of a small constant, not a $\rho$-of-cluster fraction, provided wal.sync.timeout is set comfortably above $T_{\text{roll-trigger}}$ and AsyncWAL is in use. Any abort that does occur triggers an SCP for that RS and folds back into the main recovery pipeline of §10; for $N_{\text{abort}} \lesssim 3$ the additional SCP load is dominated by ordinary noise and does not materially extend $T_{\text{recovery}}$.

HDFS-side load delta during the recovery window

The cluster-wide write rate from RegionServer WAL traffic is unchanged after AZ loss. The same offered $\lambda_{\text{wal}}$ now writes two replicas instead of three, and $333.\overline{3}$ surviving DataNodes carry it instead of $500$. The per-DataNode write rate therefore stays at $\lambda_{\text{wal}} \cdot 3 / 500 = \lambda_{\text{wal}} \cdot 2 / 333.\overline{3}$. Per-DataNode read rate is a different story. HFile and recovered-edit reads were striped across three replicas with locality preference, and now stripe across two with the same preference. The per-DN read rate climbs $\approx 50\%$:

$$ \Delta \lambda_{\text{DN-read}} \;\approx\; \frac{r}{r-1} - 1 \;=\; \frac{3}{2} - 1 \;=\; 0.5. $$

Hflush latency on a surviving DN rises because DN disks and DN handler threads are more contended, and that latency feeds directly into $\bar{h}$ for the procedure-store WAL, into $W_{\text{open}}$ for HFile opens, and into $T_{\text{WAL}}$ for split-WAL reads. NameNode throughput is sufficient and unaffected at this scale. The addBlock burst from the WAL roll cascade ($\approx 55$/s for $\approx 6\,\text{s}$) and the heartbeat-handler load from missed-heartbeat processing on $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ DataNodes (a one-off burst sized by dfs.namenode.heartbeat.recheck-interval $\approx 5\,\text{min}$ at $\approx N_{\text{dead}}/300\,\text{s} \approx 0.6$ events/s averaged, with bursts an order of magnitude higher inside the recheck window) are negligible compared to $C_{\text{NN}}$.

9.9 Limitations of the partial-failure model

Note the stale-DN write avoidance disengages when more than dfs.namenode.write.stale.datanode.ratio (default 0.5) of DNs is stale, and the slow-peer graylist cannot grow beyond dfs.namenode.max.slowpeer.collect.nodes. During an AZ-loss event the partial-failure protections are partially disabled exactly when the failure footprint is largest.

9.10 Read-path tuning for fast DataNode failover

§§9.1–9.9 cover the writer pipeline path. The read path has its own set of detection windows and timeouts, and at HDFS defaults each region open and each WAL split is exposed to a worst case 60 second pe block stall when an embedded DFSClient picks a not yet classified as dead replica. The recommendations here close that gap.

Hedged reads

DFSInputStream.hedgedFetchBlockByteRange issues the primary block read on the chosen replica, waits up to dfs.client.hedged.read.threshold.millis, and on threshold expiry submits a parallel read against another replica via the JVM-wide HEDGED_READ_THREAD_POOL. Whichever read completes first wins. The losers are cancelled. The pool size is gated by dfs.client.hedged.read.threadpool.size. At the default of 0 the path is disabled entirely (isHedgedReadsEnabled() returns false).

Effective wait under hedging is, with $F$ the per-replica latency CDF, $\theta$ the threshold, $X_1$ primary latency, and $X_2$ alt-replica latency:

$$ E[\text{wait}_{\text{hedged}}] \;=\; E[X_1 \mid X_1 < \theta]\cdot F(\theta) \;+\; E[\min(X_1,\;\theta + X_2) \mid X_1 \ge \theta]\cdot \big(1 - F(\theta)\big). $$

The threshold has to sit above the healthy P99 so it does not fire in steady state and below the slow tail values that the recovery window introduces. The per replica fire rate is $1 - F(\theta)$. Each fire issues one extra read on an alternate replica, so the hedged-read load amplification on the cross-section of surviving DNs is the same fraction.

In steady state, healthy P99 block-read latency on DataNodes sits at ${\sim}50\,\text{ms}$, comfortably below $\theta = 100\,\text{ms}$. The hedge-fire rate is $1 - F_{\text{ss}}(100\,\text{ms}) \approx 0.01$, so hedged reads add ${\lesssim}1\%$ to per-DN read load and the path is essentially dormant outside a recovery event.

In the AZ-loss window, per-DN read load rises $\approx 50\%$ (§9.8), pushing per-DN utilization from $\rho \approx 0.5$ toward $\rho \approx 0.75$ on the surviving DataNodes. Under an M/M/1 approximation the latency tail stretches by the factor $(1-\rho_{\text{ss}})/(1-\rho_{\text{AZ}}) = 0.5/0.25 = 2$, so $P_{99}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 100\text{–}125\,\text{ms}$. The hedge-fire rate climbs to $1 - F_{\text{AZ}}(100\,\text{ms}) \approx 0.05\text{–}0.10$, and the per-DN read-load envelope settles near

$$ \lambda_{\text{DN-read}}^{\text{AZ, hedged}} \;\approx\; 1.5\,\lambda_0 \cdot \big(1 + 0.05\text{–}0.10\big) \;\approx\; 1.58\text{–}1.65\,\lambda_0. $$

This sits comfortably below DataNode saturation in the reference deployment, so $\theta = 100\,\text{ms}$ is the right balance.

Per-block-read socket timeout

The client DataNode block transfer SO_TIMEOUT is dfs.client.socket-timeout (default 60000 ms, sourced from HdfsConstants.READ_TIMEOUT). The effective wait is composed with a per-pipeline-target additive in DFSClient.getDatanodeReadTimeout(numNodes):

$$ T_{\text{block-read}} \;=\; \texttt{dfs.client.socket-timeout} \;+\; \texttt{READ\_TIMEOUT\_EXTENSION} \cdot N, $$

with READ_TIMEOUT_EXTENSION = 5000 ms (a compile-time constant, not a configuration key) and $N$ the number of DataNodes the call traverses (1 for a block read). At defaults this caps a hung-DN read at $\approx 65\,\text{s}$ before the client gives up and re-chooses a replica via chooseDataNode. With hedged reads enabled this floor only matters when both replicas time out simultaneously; otherwise hedging short-circuits the wait at $\theta + p_{50,\text{alt}} \approx 150\,\text{ms}$. Reducing the floor to 15000 ms shortens the residual worst case by $4\times$ while leaving generous headroom above the recovery-window P99 of ${\sim}125\,\text{ms}$, so it does not introduce false positives on a healthy-but-momentarily-busy DN.

Cross-stream DeadNodeDetector

At HDFS defaults, dead DN tracking is per-DFSInputStream — an in-memory ConcurrentHashMap<DatanodeInfo, DatanodeInfo> built on read failure and discarded when the stream closes. Under AZ loss this means each fresh DFSInputStream rediscovers the same set of dead DataNodes independently, paying the dfs.client.socket-timeout ghost-stall once per stream per dead DN it happens to pick.

HDFS-13571 / HDFS-14542 introduced an opt-in DeadNodeDetector daemon attached to ClientContext, the per-JVM cache that holds the peer cache, short circuit fd cache, and located blocks refresher. With dfs.client.deadnode.detection.enabled = true and a single dfs.client.context name across all DFSClient instances in the JVM, the dead node set is populated exactly once on first failure and reused for every subsequent DFSInputStream. The detector probes asynchronously on its own pools (dfs.client.deadnode.detection.probe.deadnode.threads for probing dead DNs, ...probe.suspectnode.threads for newly suspect DNs, ...rpc.threads for the RPC fan-out) and removes a DN from the dead set when a probe succeeds.

9.11 NameNode RPC tuning for fast HA failover

The §8.4 “with NameNode failover” bracket of $T_{\text{NN-failover}} \approx 90\text{–}180\,\text{s}$ accounts for the HDFS side wall clock time for a ZKFC-driven HA promotion. Those timers are entirely on the HDFS side and are unchanged by anything in hbase-site.xml.

What hbase-site.xml does control is the client-side wall-clock that the embedded DFSClient in HBase Master and RegionServer JVMs spends stuck on a hung old-active before RetryPolicies.failoverOnNetworkException advances the proxy to the standby. At HDFS defaults this client-side residual can be as large as 2 minutes per stuck call. The recommendations here compress it to about 30 seconds.

Failover-proxy bridge to IPC

NameNode RPC traffic flows through a FailoverProxyProvider (typically ConfiguredFailoverProxyProvider) wrapped by RetryPolicies.failoverOnNetworkException(maxFailoverAttempts, maxRetryAttempts, sleepBase, sleepMax) at NameNodeProxiesClient.createHAProxy. The FPP’s internal AbstractNNFailoverProxyProvider reads dfs.client.failover.connection.retries and dfs.client.failover.connection.retries.on.timeouts and writes them back into each per-NN proxy’s Configuration as ipc.client.connect.max.retries and ipc.client.connect.max.retries.on.timeouts. Because both default to 0 for FPP-managed proxies, a single connect failure to one NN immediately triggers failover to the next NN, the intended fast-failover behavior. The dominant factor is the in-flight RPC timeout (ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms) rather than connect retry budgets.

Layered budget

Decompose the HBase-perceived NN-unavailable wall-clock into the HDFS-side and client-side pieces:

$$ T_{\text{NN-perceived}} \;\le\; \underbrace{T_{\text{NN-failover}}^{(\text{HDFS-side})}}_{\S\,8.4\;(90\text{--}180\,\text{s})} \;+\; \underbrace{T_{\text{client-stuck-on-hung-active}}^{(\text{client-side})}}_{\text{this subsection}}. $$

The first term is the HDFS HA wall-clock: ZKFC fencing + ZK session expiry (${\sim}30\,\text{s}$) + standby promotion (${\sim}10\text{–}20\,\text{s}$) + DN block-report quorum (${\sim}60\text{–}120\,\text{s}$). The second term is what the embedded DFSClient wastes on a hung old-active before its retry policy advances, and it splits along the failure mode of the old active. If the old active is hung but reachable (TCP handshake completes, RPC stalls), the client is bounded by ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms — default $120\,\text{s}$, tuned $30\,\text{s}$. If the old active is black-holed in the lost AZ, the client is bounded by ipc.client.connect.timeout — default $20\,\text{s}$, tuned $5\,\text{s}$ — with no connect retries, because ipc.client.connect.max.retries.on.timeouts is rewritten to 0 by the FPP bridge described above.

At HDFS defaults the client-side residual can therefore add up to ${\sim}120\,\text{s}$ to the perceived unavailability bracket. At the recommended values it adds at most ${\sim}30\,\text{s}$, so the §8.4 typical-case wall-clock estimate of $\approx 2.5\text{--}4.5\,\text{min}$ does not get a hidden ${\sim}90\,\text{s}$ pad on top.

9.12 Cross-section impact of read-path and RPC tuning

§9.8 derives the AZ-loss-window mean latency multipliers (1.5× on $\bar{h}$, $W_{\text{open}}$, and $T_{\text{WAL}}$) from the +50% per-DN read-load delta. Those numbers are unchanged here, because the read-path tuning of §9.10 does not change how much aggregate read work the surviving DataNodes carry — the same offered $\lambda_{\text{wal}}$ is striped across $N_{\text{alive}} = 333$ DNs instead of 500, so per-DN load rises $\approx 50\%$ regardless. The mean latency component is a queueing load argument and is independent of how individual readers handle the resulting tail.

What §9.10 and §9.11 do change is the worst case time an individual read or RPC can sit waiting on a not yet classified as dead replica. This subsection bounds that residual end-to-end and shows it stops contributing to the per-stage max in the recovery pipeline formula of §10.

Tail-risk decomposition

A region open or WAL split that picks an unhealthy replica can stall in three distinct ways. The first is a hung DN before timeout: a lost-AZ DataNode reachable enough to complete a TCP handshake but unresponsive to the block-read RPC, in which case the client waits up to dfs.client.socket-timeout + READ_TIMEOUT_EXTENSION $\approx 65\,\text{s}$ at HDFS defaults before retrying on a different replica. The second is a slow-but-alive DN: a surviving DataNode whose per-block read latency has slipped into the queueing-stretched tail, where P99 can reach ${\sim}500\,\text{ms}$ to several seconds once the +50% load tips the DN toward saturation. The third is black-holed NN traffic: an addBlock or getBlockLocations RPC that lands on a hung old-active NameNode and waits up to ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms $= 120\,\text{s}$ at HDFS defaults before RetryPolicies.failoverOnNetworkException advances the proxy.

The first two are addressed by §9.10, the third by §9.11. Each has a per-event bound:

$$ \begin{aligned} T_{\text{ghost-stall}}^{\text{(default)}} &\;\le\; \texttt{dfs.client.socket-timeout} \;+\; 5\,\text{s} \;\approx\; 65\,\text{s,} \\ T_{\text{ghost-stall}}^{\text{(tuned)}} &\;\le\; \theta + p_{50,\text{alt}} \;\approx\; 150\,\text{ms} \quad \text{(hedged-read short-circuit),} \\ T_{\text{NN-stuck}}^{\text{(default)}} &\;\le\; \texttt{ipc.client.rpc-timeout.ms} \;=\; 120\,\text{s,} \\ T_{\text{NN-stuck}}^{\text{(tuned)}} &\;\le\; 30\,\text{s.} \end{aligned} $$

Per-RS amortized seeding cost

Without the cross-stream DeadNodeDetector, each DFSInputStream rediscovers dead DNs independently. With $N_{\text{dead}} = 167$ and a per-block-read fail probability $p_{\text{first-pick-dead}} \approx N_{\text{dead}}/N_{\text{total}} = 0.33$, the per-stream expected stall count is $0.33 \cdot k_{\text{reads/open}}$, each costing up to dfs.client.socket-timeout. That cost multiplies across the $M_{\text{TRSP}} = 250{,}000$ region opens during recovery, with no amortization across the streams.

With DeadNodeDetector enabled and dfs.client.context pinned to a single name, the dead-node set is per-RS-process. Once the first stream in the JVM observes a failure on a dead DN, every subsequent stream skips that DN for free. The seeding cost — the wall-clock the first $N_{\text{dead}}$ stalls cost cumulatively before the detector has converged — is bounded above by

$$ T_{\text{seed}}^{\text{(per RS)}} \;\le\; \frac{N_{\text{dead}} \cdot T_{\text{ghost-stall}}}{T_{\text{open}}} \quad \text{(seeding stalls served in parallel by the per-RS open executor).} $$

Composition with the §9.8 mean-latency multipliers

The two analyses compose without double counting. The mean latency component of §9.8 — per DN read latency rising $\approx 50\%$ during recovery and inflating $\bar{h}^{\text{AZ}}$, $W_{\text{open}}^{\text{AZ}}$, and $T_{\text{WAL}}^{\text{AZ}}$ each by $\approx 1.5\times$ — is unchanged by the read path tuning of §9.10 and the NN-RPC tuning of §9.11, since neither alters the aggregate read work the surviving DataNodes carry. The worst case per-event stall residual collapses from ${\sim}65\,\text{s}$ (socket timeout bounded) or ${\sim}120\,\text{s}$ (rpc timeout bounded) to ${\sim}150\,\text{ms}$ on the read path and ${\sim}30\,\text{s}$ on the NN path, and the per-RS cost falls from ${\sim}5.6\,\text{min}$ at HDFS defaults to ${\sim}1\,\text{s}$.

The first effect is what the §4.2 multiplier table captures and what §6.6, §6.8, §6.10, §7.1 carry through their respective callouts. The second is what allows the §8.4 stressed scenario to actually settle at the $T_{\text{RS-open}} \approx 70\text{–}120\,\text{s}$ figure in the table, rather than tail-extending by minutes per dead DN that has not yet been classified out of placement decisions.

The design rule for every stage $i$ is

$$ \mu_i \;\geq\; \lambda $$

where $\mu_i$ is the per-stage service rate from Little's Law (or a burst-concurrency lower bound for serverops and the dispatcher pool, or a variance-bounded service rate for the procedure store). Total recovery latency is governed by the drain time of the slowest stage:

$$ T_{\text{recovery}} \;=\; \max_i \frac{W_{\text{total},i}}{\mu_i}, $$

where $W_{\text{total},i}$ is the aggregate workload routed through stage $i$ during the recovery window (e.g. $W_{\text{total}}$ procedure state transitions through the procedure store, $M_{\text{TRSP}}$ region opens through the per-RS open executor). This is the fluid flow drain time, distinct from the Little's Law concurrency identity $L = \lambda W$ used in §5 to size individual thread pools. Minimizing $T_{\text{recovery}}$ requires balancing service rates across stages, not maximizing any single one. The three layers must be tuned together.

During the AZ-loss window each stage's $\mu_i$ is the derated value from §9.8, summarized in the §4.2 multiplier table. At the reference workload the procedure store remains the bottlenecked stage, with $T_{\text{proc-drain}}^{\text{AZ}} \approx 73\,\text{s}$.

The HDFS writer pipeline tuning of §9 has read and write side changes that must be applied together. The write side changes (§9.6, §9.7) bound the cost of pipeline recovery on a sick or dead DataNode and is what keeps the WAL writer pipelines from collapsing during the AZ loss window. The read side changes (§9.10) bound the tail latency of block reads on the HFile open and WAL split paths via hedged reads + per-block-read socket timeout + JVM-shared DeadNodeDetector, and the NN RPC changes (§9.11) bound the time the embedded DFSClient spends stuck on a dead NameNode during HA failover. The mean latency component (§9.8) and the tail -risk component (§9.12) compose without double counting. The first inflates each stage's $\mu_i$ by $\approx 1.5\times$, the second prevents catastrophic per-event stalls from elevating any single stage's $T_{\text{stage}}$ above the ceiling established by procedure store drain.

11. Limitations of the model

The procedure store sizing accounts for service time variance via a bulk service queueing argument (§7). The remaining first order simplifications:

HDFS treated as a single ops/sec ceiling

The NameNode is modeled as $C_{\text{NN}}$ ops/sec with no internal structure. Real NameNode behavior includes a global lock that distinguishes read paths from write paths. Under read-heavy bursts (region opens) the NN can sustain higher op rates than under mixed load. DataNode disk-read concurrency is also not modeled. HFile footer/index/bloom reads compete with normal foreground reads on the same DNs.

RS-side $W_{\text{open}}$ is a free parameter

Per-region open time is treated as an exogenous input ranging from 200 ms (healthy) to 5 s (stressed), but it is in fact a function of the number of HFiles per region, the number of recovered edits, NameNode latency, and DataNode block-cache state. A more complete model would derive $W_{\text{open}}$ from these inputs rather than parameterize it.

No contention modeling

Region locks, META row locks, and the procedure-store single-writer all serialize work; the model treats each as an ideal single-server queue. Lock contention between concurrent TRSPs sharing a parent SCP is not represented.

No retry amplification

Dispatcher retries on transient failures and RS-side RegionAlreadyInTransitionException retries are absorbed into "headroom" rather than modeled as $\lambda_{\text{eff}} = \lambda(1 + r)$ for a per-RPC retry rate $r$.

No coupling between META writes and the procedure WAL

Both are single-writer log-structured stores. In reality their hflush streams compete for the same NameNode and DataNode pipeline capacity but the model treats them as independent.

No feedback from queue depth back to arrival

Heartbeats from surviving RSs are treated as an exogenous arrival stream. In reality, master overload increases heartbeat-handling latency, which can feed back into RS-side timeouts and retries — a self-amplifying mode the linear model does not capture.

Recovered edits replay not modeled separately

Under AZ loss, regions inherit recovered edits from split WALs that were owned by the dead RSs. Replay during region open is its own HDFS-bound and CPU-bound workload that the per-region open-time abstraction $W_{\text{open}}$ collapses into a single number.

AsyncWAL is assumed throughout

The HDFS-side and cross-section derivations from §4 onward assume the AsyncWAL provider (hbase.wal.provider = asyncfs). §9.3 documents the FSHLog mechanics for reference but does not extend the cross-section math to that case.

12. Future work

Core/overflow dynamic thread-pool sizing

The static thread counts in §8.1–§8.2 are sized to absorb the worst-case AZ-loss burst, but the master pays for that capacity in stack memory, scheduler bookkeeping, and JVM working-set pressure proportional to the static cap. A core/overflow pool model would let the cluster run at a lower idle thread footprint while still hitting the same recovery bounds during a storm. Under this model the values in §8.1 become the upper bound of the overflow tier rather than the always-on thread count. The procedure executor pool is the highest leverage candidate ($N_{\text{proc}} = 512$ at static sizing). The burst-driven master executors (serverops, openregion, closeregion, metahandler) are next, since their utilization outside a recovery storm is near zero.

Procedure-store / META coupling

Both the procedure store WAL and the META region are single-writer log structured stores whose hflush streams compete for the same NameNode and DataNode pipeline capacity (§11). Folding the coupling in would let the procedure store / META interaction on $\bar{h}$ become a first class prediction rather than an independence assumption.
